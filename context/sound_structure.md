# The Sound Structure of Language

Reference for the high-level properties of sound systems: inventories,
contrasts, patterns, and variation across languages. Used by skills that
need to situate a specific question inside the bigger picture of why
phonetics matters.

---

## Sound inventory

Every spoken language has a **sound inventory**: the set of distinct
speech sounds (phonemes) that language uses.

- All spoken languages have both **vowels** and **consonants**.
- Inventory size varies hugely: from about 11 consonants in Rotokas or
  Pirahã, up to around 140 sounds in !Xóõ.
- English uses roughly 24 consonants and somewhere between 11–15 vowels
  depending on dialect.

No language uses every possible human speech sound — each language
selects a subset. The subset isn't random. Three factors shape which
sounds tend to appear together:

1. **Articulatory ease.** Sounds that are similar in articulation are
   easier to produce in sequence. If a language has *p*, it's likely to
   also have *b* and *m* (same lip gesture).
2. **Perceptual distinctiveness.** Sounds that listeners can easily
   tell apart support clear communication. A language is unlikely to
   have two sounds that are nearly acoustically identical.
3. **Economic use of gestures.** The same articulatory gestures —
   movements of tongue, lips, velum — get reused for many sounds.
   Efficient systems don't invent a new gesture for every sound.

## Sound contrasts

A **contrast** is a distinction between sounds that can change the
meaning of a word.

- *bit* vs. *bat* — the vowel is different, and so is the meaning.
- *tip* vs. *dip* — the only difference is voicing on the first
  consonant, and that's enough to distinguish two words.

When two sounds can contrast words like this, they are separate
**phonemes** in the language. Phonemes are the building blocks that
sound inventories are built from.

## Sound patterns

Sounds don't just exist as an inventory — they combine in patterned
ways to make up words. Every language has rules (often implicit) about
which combinations are allowed and which aren't.

### English examples

Some consonant clusters are allowed at the start of a word:

- *pl-* in *play*, *pr-* in *pray*, *st-* in *stop*

Others aren't:

- *pt-*, *tl-*, *vr-* — impossible in English word-initial position.

But the same sequences can appear elsewhere in the word:

- *pt* is fine in the middle of a word: *apt*, *kept*, *helicopter*.

And English speakers know these patterns implicitly. Given a novel word
like *flabble* or *prlauiop*, English speakers readily classify the
first as possible, the second as impossible — without ever having been
taught rules about what's possible.

### Borrowings reveal patterns

When speakers borrow words from other languages with different
patterns, they often adjust the word to fit their own system:

- Greek *Ptolemy* → English speakers often say [tɒləmi], dropping
  the initial *p*.
- English *special*, *sprite* → Spanish speakers often say *especial*,
  *esprite*, inserting a vowel so the word starts with a legal cluster.

This tells us that sound patterns aren't just abstract rules — they
shape what speakers actually do.

## Beyond consonants and vowels: prosody

Speech has more structure than just a sequence of consonants and vowels.
**Prosody** is the "music of speech": rhythm, stress, pitch, intonation,
tone.

- **Intonation**: the pitch contour of a phrase. In English, the same
  words can be a statement or a question depending on intonation
  — *I'm late.* vs. *I'm late?*
- **Tone**: in some languages, pitch on individual syllables changes
  word meaning. Thai is a classic example:

  | Tone              | Example | Meaning     |
  |-------------------|---------|-------------|
  | Low falling       | *nà:*   | (a name)    |
  | High falling      | *nâ:*   | "face"      |
  | High rising       | *ná:*   | "aunt"      |
  | Low falling-rising| *nǎ:*   | "thick"     |
  | Mid falling       | *na:*   | "field"     |

## Cross-linguistic variation

Thousands of languages exist in the world (estimates around 6,500, many
endangered). They vary enormously in what sounds they use:

- About 200 distinct vowel sounds and 600+ distinct consonant sounds
  have been documented across the world's languages.
- Many English sounds are unusual from the perspective of other
  languages (the *th* sounds [θ] and [ð] are fairly rare).
- Many languages have sounds that don't appear in English at all
  (clicks in Zulu and Xhosa, pharyngeal fricatives in Arabic,
  phonemic retroflexion in Hindi).

The goal of phonetics is to describe and classify all of these sounds
using a universally applicable framework — one that isn't tied to any
particular language's spelling or conventions. That's what the IPA is
for, and why we insist on setting English orthography aside when doing
phonetic transcription.
