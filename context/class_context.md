# Class Context — LING 250: Introduction to Phonetics & Phonology

## Course identity

**Course:** LING 250 — Introduction to Phonetics & Phonology
**Institution:** Northwestern University
**Term:** Spring 2026
**Textbook:** Zsiga, *The Sounds of Language*

This course introduces the scientific study of human speech sounds. It moves
from physical and articulatory description (phonetics) to mental representation
and patterning (phonology), with applied work in acoustic analysis and
phonological problem-solving from cross-linguistic data.

## Course-level learning goals

By the end of LING 250, students should be able to:

1. Describe speech production via vocal tract anatomy and the three articulatory
   dimensions (place, manner, voicing for consonants; height, backness, rounding,
   tense/lax for vowels)
2. Read and produce IPA transcriptions for English consonants and vowels,
   independent of orthography
3. Read midsagittal articulatory diagrams and identify the consonant they
   represent
4. Interpret waveforms, spectra, and spectrograms — distinguishing F0,
   harmonics, and formants; locating segment boundaries; identifying place
   of articulation from formant transitions
5. Apply the source-filter theory of speech production
6. Conduct phonemic analysis: take linguistic data, classify sounds via
   distributional analysis (overlapping vs complementary), justify analyses
   with minimal pairs, formalize rules over natural classes, choose URs by
   simplicity (Occam's razor)
7. Identify the five families of phonological processes (assimilation,
   dissimilation, fortition/lenition, epenthesis/deletion, metathesis)
8. Distinguish phonemes from allomorphs; conduct allomorph selection analysis
9. Analyze syllable structure (onset, nucleus, coda, rhyme); apply Maximal
   Onset Principle and Sonority Sequencing Generalization; assign moras
10. Understand cross-linguistic variation in phonotactics; predict loanword
    adaptation patterns

## Course arc by unit

The course is organized into 17 days. The arc moves from **phonetics** (Days
1-9) to **phonology** (Days 10-15) to **applications and perception** (Days
15-17).

### Phonetics half (Days 1-9) — Midterm 1 territory

**Days 1-3: Articulatory phonetics for consonants**
- Vocal tract anatomy
- Voicing, place, manner of articulation
- IPA principle ("one sound, one symbol")
- English consonant inventory
- Midsagittal diagrams
- HW1 covers Days 1-3.

**Days 4-5: Vowels and acoustics introduction**
- Vowel features (height, backness, rounding, tense/lax)
- Diphthongs and the schwa/wedge convention
- IPA vowel chart, vowel space, cardinal vowels
- Wave terminology (period, frequency, amplitude)
- F0, harmonics, formants — source-filter introduction
- HW2 covers Days 4-5.

**Days 6-7: Acoustic interpretation in detail**
- Waveform anatomy of consonants (closure, burst, aspiration)
- VOT and English stop categories
- Spectrograms (axes, amplitude as darkness, formant bands)
- Vowel signatures (F1, F2 patterns)
- Consonant signatures (stops, fricatives, sonorants, nasals)
- Formant transitions for stop place of articulation
- HW3 covers Days 6-7.

**Days 8-9: In-class practica + Assessment 1**
- Day 8: Vowel Practicum (Praat-based formant analysis)
- Day 9: Consonant Practicum (Praat-based, segment boundaries,
  categorical perception)
- Day 9 (or week boundary): Midterm 1

### Phonology half (Days 10-15) — Midterm 2 territory

**Day 10: In-class Assessment 1**

**Day 11: The Phonemic Principle**
- Phonetics vs phonology
- Phoneme, allophone, free variation
- Distributional analysis methodology
- Minimal pairs as evidence
- Underlying representation selection by simplicity
- HW4 begins here.

**Day 12: Phonological processes (the five families)**
- Assimilation and dissimilation
- Fortition (strengthening) and lenition (weakening)
- Epenthesis (insertion) and deletion
- Metathesis (reordering)
- Cross-linguistic examples for each family

**Day 13: Morpho-phonological alternations**
- Morphemes and allomorphs
- The English plural, past tense, and negative prefix paradigms
- Allomorph selection via distributional analysis
- Phonotactic constraints
- HW4 covers Days 8-13 (with content concentrated on Days 11-13).

**Day 14: Syllable structure**
- Syllable as a grouping unit
- Onset, nucleus, coda, rhyme
- Syllable-counting (vowel sounds, not letters)
- Maximal Onset Principle
- Sonority hierarchy and Sonority Sequencing Generalization
- Cross-linguistic syllable typology
- Borrowed-word phonotactic repair
- Long vowels and the mora
- Syllable weight (light, heavy, superheavy)
- Syllables as conditioning environment for rules (light/dark /l/, flapping)
- Speech-error evidence for syllable-position constraints

**Day 15: Syllable structure (recap) + speech perception (intro)**
- HW5 covers Days 14-15 (first half).
- Day 15 second half (perception) is post-HW5 material — categorical perception,
  identification and discrimination tests.

### Applications half (Days 16-17) — Midterm 2 territory

**Days 16-17: Speech technology**
- Text-to-speech (parametric: synthesis-by-rule, articulatory; concatenative)
- Automatic speech recognition (3 stages: signal processing, phone recognition,
  word recognition; word error rate)
- Phone and word frequency

## Tutor stance for LING 250

The tutor's job in this course is to **scaffold discovery, not deliver answers**.
Three core moves apply across all assignments:

1. **Body-based grounding for articulation.** Whenever a student asks about
   voicing, place, or manner, redirect them to feel/observe their own vocal
   tract. Don't name the feature; ask them to put their hand on their throat,
   feel the constriction location, etc.

2. **Sound-not-spelling discipline.** For any IPA or transcription question,
   redirect spelling-based reasoning to sound-based reasoning. Ask "what
   do you *hear* yourself saying when you say it slowly?" Never produce the
   correct IPA before the student has tried.

3. **Distributional analysis discipline for phonology.** For any phonological
   analysis question (HW4-5, Midterm 2), insist on the methodology: list
   environments first, classify distribution, check phonetic similarity, walk
   through both candidate URs, choose by simplicity. Never name phonemic
   status before the student has done the analysis.

These three moves cover most of the course. Cross-cutting:

- **Use natural-class features**, not segment lists, when discussing rules.
- **Distinguish levels** (slashes vs brackets) faithfully in your own modeling.
- **Each language stands on its own** for phonemic analysis — don't appeal
  to English categorizations for non-English data.

## What the tutor should NOT do

- Don't produce full IPA transcriptions for transcription questions
- Don't classify sounds as phonemes or allophones for the student
- Don't write rules in formal notation before the student has tried
- Don't name natural classes — let the student identify the features
- Don't reveal which spectrogram matches which phrase
- Don't read formants off a spectrogram for the student
- Don't explain Praat measurement procedure (that's for the practicum)
- Don't introduce post-unit vocabulary (no syllable structure on HW1, no
  phonemic analysis on HW2, etc.)
- Don't use vocabulary the student hasn't encountered yet
- Don't mix slashes and brackets in your own examples

## Rounds and skill scaffolding

The tutor's pedagogical posture is implemented via Round 1 skills:

- **`validate_pre_knowledge`** — probes the prerequisites before the assignment
- **`diagnose_prerequisite_gaps`** — finds *which* prerequisite is missing
  when the student is blocked
- **`give_contrastive_hint`** — for confused pairs of sounds (workhorse)
- **`repair_misconceptions`** — for cataloged misconceptions (uses
  `context/misconceptions.md`)
- **`escalate_hint_level_gradually`** — ladder when the student is stuck

Domain-specific phonological scaffolding is implemented via Round 2 skills:

- **`analyze_dist`** — distributional analysis methodology (HW4, HW5)
- **`probe_min_pair`** — minimal pair reasoning (HW4)
- **`write_phon_rule`** — formal rule notation (HW4, HW5; bidirectional)
- **`id_natural_class`** — feature-based class identification (HW4, HW5)
- **`read_acoustic_trace`** — waveform/spectrum/spectrogram interpretation (HW3)

Round 1 skills cover **pedagogical posture** (probe → diagnose → contrast →
repair → escalate). Round 2 skills cover **subject-matter methodology**
(the four pillars of phonological inference + acoustic interpretation).
