# The Vocal Tract and Speech Production

Reference for how speech is produced anatomically: airflow from lungs to
lips, the three subsystems of the vocal tract, and the landmarks that
speech sounds are classified by. Used by skills that need to ground
students' intuitions about articulation in anatomy.

---

## Speech is the movement of air made audible

All speech — in any language — starts with an airstream. In English and
most languages, that airstream comes from the lungs and flows outward
through the vocal tract. Along the way, it gets shaped by the larynx,
the tongue, the lips, and the soft palate to produce distinct speech
sounds.

The big picture: **air moves, something constricts it, the shape of the
constriction determines what sound comes out.**

---

## Three subsystems

The vocal tract is divided into three regions based on where they sit
relative to the larynx (the "voice box"):

### 1. Sub-laryngeal system (below the larynx)
- **Components:** lungs, trachea
- **Function:** provides the airstream — the raw material of speech
- Nothing that distinguishes one speech sound from another happens here;
  the sub-laryngeal system just supplies the air.

### 2. Laryngeal system (the larynx itself)
- **Components:** larynx, vocal cords (vocal folds), glottis, epiglottis
- **Function:** controls voicing and pitch
- This is where **voicing** is produced. When the vocal folds vibrate,
  the sound is voiced (*z*, *b*, *m*). When they don't, the sound is
  voiceless (*s*, *p*, *f*).

### 3. Supra-laryngeal vocal tract (above the larynx)
- **Three cavities:** pharynx, oral cavity, nasal cavity
- **Function:** shapes the airstream into distinct speech sounds
- This is where **place** and **manner** of articulation happen.

## Key landmarks

These are the anatomical parts you'll hear referenced constantly:

| Part              | What it is                                                    |
|-------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| **glottis**       | the opening between the vocal cords                           |
| **larynx**        | the "voice box" where voicing is controlled                   |
| **pharynx**       | tubular throat above the larynx                               |
| **oral cavity**   | the mouth                                                     |
| **nasal cavity**  | the nose and connecting passages                              |
| **velum**         | soft palate; raising/lowering it opens or closes the nasal passage |
| **hard palate**   | the hard roof of the mouth                                    |
| **alveolar ridge**| the bony ridge just behind the upper teeth                    |
| **tongue tip**    | the very front edge of the tongue                             |
| **tongue blade**  | the part just behind the tip                                  |
| **tongue body**   | the main mass of the tongue                                   |
| **lips**          | upper and lower; can close, approach, or round               |

## How a speech sound is made

Two things determine what sound comes out:

1. **The airstream** — is the air flowing, and are the vocal folds
   vibrating (voicing)?
2. **The constriction** — where in the vocal tract is the airstream
   narrowed or blocked (place), and how tightly (manner)?

This is why every English consonant can be described by three features:

- **Voicing:** voiced or voiceless
- **Place of articulation:** where the constriction happens
- **Manner of articulation:** what kind of constriction it is

Vowels are produced with the vocal tract relatively open — the tongue
and lips shape the cavity, but nothing obstructs the airflow.

---

## Vowels vs. consonants (in one line)

- **Vowel:** little to no constriction; airflow is unobstructed.
- **Consonant:** significant constriction — a narrow channel, a near
  closure, or a total closure.
