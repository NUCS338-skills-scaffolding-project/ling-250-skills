# Common Phonetics Misconceptions

A list of wrong mental models students frequently bring to Ling 250.
Each entry has: the misconception, why it's wrong, and a brief correction.

Used by the `repair_misconceptions` skill to recognize and address
student confusions through questioning rather than direct correction.

---

## 1. Spelling reflects pronunciation

**Misconception:** You can figure out how a word sounds from how it's spelled.

**Why it's wrong:** English orthography is notoriously inconsistent. The same letter represents different sounds (*c* in *cat* vs *cell*), the same sound is spelled different ways (*sh* in *ship*, *ti* in *nation*, *ci* in *special*), and sounds appear that aren't written (the glottal stop in *uh-oh*).

**Correction target:** Ask the student to find two words where the same letter is pronounced differently — or the same sound is spelled differently.

---

## 2. "Th" is one sound

**Misconception:** The letters *th* represent a single sound.

**Why it's wrong:** *Th* represents two different sounds: voiceless [θ] as in *thing*, and voiced [ð] as in *this*. The only difference is voicing.

**Correction target:** Ask the student to say *thing* and *this* with a hand on their throat and notice where they feel vibration.

---

## 3. "Ch" is a simple sound

**Misconception:** *Ch* as in *chip* is a single consonant like [t] or [s].

**Why it's wrong:** [tʃ] is an affricate — it begins as a complete stop closure ([t]) and releases into a fricative ([ʃ]). Two articulations in one symbol.

**Correction target:** Ask the student to say *chip* very slowly and notice what their tongue does at the very beginning versus what comes out after.

---

## 4. "Y" is always a vowel

**Misconception:** *Y* is a vowel (or "sometimes a vowel").

**Why it's wrong:** The letter *y* can represent either the vowel [i] (*happy*) or the consonant [j] — a palatal approximant (*yes*). It's the sound that matters, not the letter.

**Correction target:** Ask the student to compare the beginning of *yes* with the beginning of *in*. What's different about what the tongue is doing?

---

## 5. Nasals only involve the nose

**Misconception:** Nasal sounds are produced only in the nose.

**Why it's wrong:** Nasals involve a complete closure in the oral cavity AND a lowered velum. Air escapes through the nose because it can't escape through the mouth. The oral closure is what distinguishes [m] from [n] from [ŋ].

**Correction target:** Ask the student what their lips, tongue tip, and tongue back are doing during [m], [n], and [ŋ].

---

## 6. Voiceless sounds are silent

**Misconception:** If a sound is "voiceless," it means no sound is made.

**Why it's wrong:** Voiceless means the vocal folds aren't vibrating. There's still plenty of audible sound — friction in fricatives, bursts in stops, etc. *Voiceless* ≠ *silent*.

**Correction target:** Ask the student to whisper a sentence. Is it silent? What's making the sound?

---

## 7. [ŋ] is two sounds

**Misconception:** The *ng* at the end of *sing* is [n] followed by [g].

**Why it's wrong:** [ŋ] is a single velar nasal. In words like *sing*, there's no [g] sound at all — just [ŋ]. (Though in words like *finger*, both [ŋ] and [g] are present.)

**Correction target:** Ask the student to say *sing* and then *finger* slowly. Where is the tongue at the end of each?

---

## 8. Affricates are fast sequences of stop + fricative

**Misconception:** [tʃ] is just [t] followed quickly by [ʃ].

**Why it's wrong:** While the articulation is stop-then-fricative, affricates behave as single units phonologically. They pattern like single consonants in terms of syllable structure and timing, not like consonant clusters.

**Correction target:** Ask the student to compare *catch it* [kætʃ ɪt] with *cat ship* [kæt ʃɪp]. Do these sound different? Why?

---
## 9. English "r" is the IPA [r]

**Misconception:** The IPA symbol [r] is the English r sound in *red*, *run*, *very*.

**Why it's wrong:** [r] in IPA is a **trill** — a series of rapid taps from the tongue tip against the alveolar ridge. English does NOT use the trill. The English r is the **rhotic approximant [ɹ]**, a single approximation without contact. Spanish *que rico*, Italian *rosso*, and Scottish *bright red* use the trill [r].

**Correction target:** Ask the student to say *red* in English, then try saying *rico* the way a native Spanish speaker would. What is your tongue doing differently?

---

## 10. Doubled consonant letters are pronounced as the doubled letter

**Misconception:** The "tt" in *butter* is pronounced [t] because that's the letter.

**Why it's wrong:** In American English, *butter* is pronounced [bʌɾəɹ] with a tap [ɾ], not [t]. The tap appears between vowels when the first vowel is stressed and the second is unstressed: *butter*, *water*, *ladder*, *city*, *better*. The "tt"/"dd" spelling doesn't change — the environment does. Outside that environment (e.g., stressed second syllable, *attest*) the underlying [t] surfaces.

**Correction target:** Ask the student to say *butter* and *attest* slowly, paying attention to what the tongue does at the middle consonant. Are they the same?

---

## 11. The IPA chart is just a list

**Misconception:** The IPA chart is a flat list of symbols you have to memorize one by one.

**Why it's wrong:** The chart is **organized**. Columns are place of articulation (left = front of mouth, right = back). Rows are manner of articulation. When two symbols share a cell, they differ only in voicing — voiceless on the left, voiced on the right. Once you know the structure, you can look up any consonant by its three features rather than memorizing each symbol.

**Correction target:** Ask the student to find the cell for "alveolar fricative" on the chart. What two symbols are there? What's the only difference between them?

---

## 12. The formal description of a consonant always names all three features

**Misconception:** To describe a consonant formally, you always have to say voicing AND place AND manner — e.g., "voiced bilabial nasal stop."

**Why it's wrong:** Predictable features are conventionally omitted. Since all English nasals are voiced and have complete oral closure, [m] is described as "bilabial nasal" — not "voiced bilabial nasal stop." The convention is to mention only the features that distinguish a sound from others sharing the same context. (See Day 3 slide 18.)

**Correction target:** Ask the student: "What does saying 'voiced' add to the description of [m]? Is there a voiceless [m] in English?"
