# Common Misconceptions — LING 250

This is the course-wide misconceptions catalog. Skills like `repair_misconceptions`
draw from this catalog when a student's answer or framing reveals one of these patterns.

Misconceptions are **organized by domain** (the territory where they apply), not
by which HW exposes them. Most misconceptions appear across multiple assignments.
The "Tested in" line lists where each one shows up so the orchestrator can match
in-session signals to catalog entries.

Priority levels:
- **CRITICAL** — recurring high-cost errors observed across multiple HWs/quizzes
- **HIGH** — common error pattern, tested explicitly on at least one HW or midterm
- **MEDIUM** — appears in one or two contexts, worth catching when it surfaces
- **LOW** — peripheral or rarely surfaced

---

## Domain 1: Orthography vs Phonetics

### M1.1 Spelling reflects pronunciation [CRITICAL]
Tested in: HW1 Q1 + every transcription question; HW2 Q1-Q6 (multi-select feature
identification); Make-up HW Q1, Q3, Q11.

Students write the IPA symbol that matches the English *letter* rather than the
*sound*. Example: writing [c] for the first sound of *cell* (correct: [s]); writing
[s] for the final sound of *raised* (correct: [z]).

**Repair move:** ask the student to say the word slowly aloud, then ask "what do
you *hear* yourself saying?" Never name the correct sound directly.

### M1.2 "Th" is one sound [HIGH]
Tested in: HW1 Q11; any transcription with *th*.

Students fail to distinguish [θ] (voiceless, *think*) from [ð] (voiced, *this*).

**Repair move:** ask the student to put their hand on their throat, say *think*
then *this*, and feel which one buzzes.

### M1.3 "Ch" is a simple consonant, not an affricate [HIGH]
Tested in: HW1 Q4, Q18, Q30 (transcription of *reached*).

Students think [tʃ] is one sound, missing the stop+fricative structure.

**Repair move:** ask the student to say *cheese* very slowly. They'll naturally
produce [t] then [ʃ] separately at slow rate.

### M1.4 "Y" is always a vowel [HIGH]
Tested in: HW1 Q22 (*yellow* → [jɛlo]).

Students treat [j] as a vowel symbol because of the "y" letter.

### M1.5 "[ŋ]" is two sounds [HIGH]
Tested in: HW1 Q11; any transcription with word-final *ng*.

The "ng" digraph spells one sound. Students often write [ng] (two segments).

### M1.6 English r is the IPA [r] [HIGH]
Tested in: HW1 Day 3 slides + any English transcription.

[r] is a trill (Spanish/Italian). English uses [ɹ] (alveolar approximant).

### M1.7 Doubled consonant letters spell doubled sounds [HIGH]
Tested in: HW1 Q26 (*wouldn't*); medial *tt*, *dd* (*butter*, *water*, *ladder*).

The doubling is orthographic, not phonological. Medial *tt*/*dd* between vowels
is a tap [ɾ], not [t]/[d].

## Domain 2: Vowel Articulation

### M2.1 Tense and lax differ only in length [CRITICAL]
Tested in: HW2 Q5, Q6 (multi-select); HW2 Q19-Q20 (vowel transformation).

Students think [i] vs [ɪ] is "long i vs short i." It's a **quality difference**
(tongue body position) plus length, with quality dominant. *Beat* and *bit*
don't rhyme even at identical durations.

**Repair move:** ask the student to say *beat* then *bit* and feel where the
tongue body sits. Different position, not just different duration.

### M2.2 Schwa and wedge are different vowels [HIGH]
Tested in: HW1 Day 4 slides; HW2 Q26 (sentence transcription); Make-up HW Q4.

Schwa [ə] and wedge [ʌ] share quality (central-mid). They're distributed by
**stress**: schwa unstressed, wedge stressed. Students who treat them as fully
separate phonemes will over-distinguish.

### M2.3 Schwa appears in stressed syllables [HIGH — INVERTED]
Tested in: Make-up HW Q4.

Inverted from M2.2: students sometimes invert the convention. Schwa = unstressed,
wedge = stressed.

### M2.4 Diphthongs are two vowels [HIGH]
Tested in: HW2 Q7 (diphthong matching); HW2 Q26.

Diphthongs are *single* vowels with changing quality during their production.
Counting them as two leads to wrong syllable structure analysis (HW5).

### M2.5 Lax vowels can occur word-finally [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW2 Q26 (composed sentence).

In English, lax vowels don't occur word-finally (except schwa). Useful diagnostic
for tense/lax classification when uncertain.

### M2.6 The vowel chart shows literal tongue positions [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW2 Q23-Q25 (cardinal vowels).

The chart is an *idealized* schematic. Real tongue positions vary across
speakers and languages.

### M2.7 Cardinal vowels are the most common world vowel inventory [CRITICAL]
Tested in: HW2 Q23 (Lucille missed); Quiz 1 Q3, Quiz 1 Q5.

The most common 5-vowel inventory is [i, e, u, o, a] (Spanish-style). Cardinal
vowels are an idealized Daniel Jones reference set, NOT typological. Students
project them as universal.

**Repair move:** name the contrast. "Cardinal vowels are reference points used
by linguists to describe any language's vowels. They are not the vowels of any
particular language."

### M2.8 Cardinal vowels 1-4 are not auditorily equidistant [HIGH]
Tested in: HW2 Q24 (Lucille missed).

They ARE. The defining property of cardinals is auditory equidistance.

### M2.9 All languages have the same vowel features as English [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Day 4 slides cross-linguistic notes.

Tone, nasality, length, and voice quality are contrastive in many languages
but not in English (~70% of world's languages have tone).

## Domain 3: Acoustics & Source-Filter

### M3.1 F0 and F1 are the same thing [CRITICAL]
Tested in: HW3 Q7-Q8 (Lucille missed); Make-up HW Q7-Q8.

F0 = fundamental frequency, the rate of vocal fold vibration; perceived as pitch.
F1 = first formant, the lowest amplified resonance of the vocal tract.
**Different physical quantities. Different perceptual cues.**

**Repair move:** start with the source-filter contrast. F0 lives in the source
(vocal folds). F1 is one of the formants — products of the filter (vocal tract
shape). They're measured differently and they vary independently.

### M3.2 Harmonics and formants are the same [CRITICAL]
Tested in: HW3 Q4-Q5; Make-up HW Q8.

Harmonics are integer multiples of F0 (purely a property of the source).
Formants are the *subset of harmonics that the vocal tract amplifies* (a
property of the filter). Every vowel has the same harmonic series given fixed
F0; what differs across vowels is *which* harmonics become formant peaks.

### M3.3 Higher F0 → narrower harmonic spacing [HIGH — INVERTED]
Tested in: HW3 Q2 (Lucille missed).

INVERTED. **Higher F0 → wider harmonic spacing.** Harmonics at integer multiples
of F0; doubling F0 doubles the spacing. F0=150 → harmonics at 150, 300, 450...
F0=300 → harmonics at 300, 600, 900...

### M3.4 F1 corresponds to vowel backness [HIGH — INVERTED]
Tested in: HW3 Q3, Q10-Q15; Make-up HW Q9-Q10.

INVERTED. **F1 ↔ height; F2 ↔ backness.**

### M3.5 High vowels have HIGH F1 [CRITICAL — INVERTED]
Tested in: HW3 spectrum reading; Make-up HW Q10 (Lucille missed).

INVERTED. **High vowels have LOW F1.** Low vowels have high F1.

Reasoning from articulation: a low vowel has an open jaw, which shrinks the
pharynx, which raises the first resonance.

**Repair move:** "Counterintuitive but consistent. The trick: F1 measures
something about the *opposite* end of the mouth from what 'high' suggests.
High tongue → small mouth opening → constrained F1."

### M3.6 Amplitude equals loudness [HIGH]
Tested in: HW2 Q28; Make-up HW Q5.

Loudness is *psychophysical* — depends on intensity (which depends on amplitude
AND frequency AND medium). Logarithmic, not linear. Doubling amplitude doesn't
double loudness.

### M3.7 Frequency equals pitch [HIGH]
Tested in: HW2 Q30; HW2 Q27.

Same shape as amplitude/loudness. Roughly proportional but not identical,
especially at extremes.

### M3.8 Frequency and amplitude are directly related [HIGH]
Tested in: Make-up HW Q5.

They're **independent**. A 100 Hz sine can be loud or quiet; a 500 Hz sine
can be loud or quiet, regardless of frequency.

### M3.9 A "complex wave" has a complicated F0-amplitude relationship [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Make-up HW Q6.

A complex wave is **a wave made up of more than one frequency**. Speech is
complex; pure tones are not. The word "complex" doesn't refer to dynamics or
modulation.

### M3.10 F0 changes affect formants [HIGH]
Tested in: HW3 Q1-Q3; Day 7 slides.

They don't. F0 is a property of the source; formants are properties of the
filter. **Same vowel at different F0 = same formants, different harmonic
spacing.**

### M3.11 Vowels are simple sounds [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Day 5 acoustics intro.

Vowels are *complex* periodic waves — superpositions of many sine waves.
Students who model them as pure tones can't make sense of formants.

### M3.12 The vocal tract is the source of speech sound [HIGH]
Tested in: Quiz 2 Q3.

The vocal *folds* are the source. The vocal *tract* is the filter. Source-
filter confusion blocks most spectrogram interpretation.

### M3.13 All vowel inventories have ~5 vowels like Spanish/Japanese [LOW]
Tested in: Day 4 cross-linguistic notes.

Inventories range from 3 (some Australian languages) to 20+ (some Germanic).
English has 13-15 monophthongs depending on dialect, plus 5 diphthongs.

## Domain 4: Waveforms & Spectrograms (HW3)

### M4.1 VOT is the same as voicing [HIGH]
Tested in: HW3 Q1; Quiz 3 Q1.

VOT is a *timing* measure (continuous), not a binary feature. English /b d g/
can have negative, zero, or short-lag VOT depending on context, while still
being phonemically "voiced."

### M4.2 All English voiced stops are prevoiced [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Quiz 3 Q1.

In phrase-initial position, English /b d g/ are typically zero / short-lag,
NOT negative. Prevoicing is reliable in intervocalic position. Spanish *does*
consistently prevoice — which is why English speakers' "voiced" stops sound
"voiceless" to Spanish ears.

### M4.3 Aspiration is part of the consonant [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Day 6 slides; Q1 implicit.

Aspiration is the *delay in voicing onset* after the stop release — an artifact
of the closure-to-vowel transition, not an extra "puff" attached to the
consonant.

### M4.4 Stops have constant amplitude [LOW]
Tested in: HW3 Q18 (segment boundary identification).

Stops have multiple distinct phases (closure → burst → release/aspiration),
each with very different amplitude. Reading a stop as a single block obscures
the diagnostic features.

### M4.5 Voiceless fricatives have no waveform [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW3 Q18.

They have a clear waveform; it's just *aperiodic*. The waveform shows the
high-frequency noise of the friction.

### M4.6 Darkness on a spectrogram is frequency [HIGH]
Tested in: HW3 Q6; Make-up HW.

Darkness is *amplitude* (intensity at that frequency, at that time). Frequency
is on the y-axis.

### M4.7 Place of articulation can be read directly from the consonant on a spectrogram [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Day 7 slides; HW3 Q16.

For nasals and stops, place is read from the *adjacent vowel's* formant
transitions, not from the consonant itself. Counterintuitive.

### M4.8 All speakers have the same formant values for the same vowel [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW3 Q9.

Different speakers have F1, F2 differences of several hundred Hz for "the same"
vowel because of vocal tract length differences. Comparing across speakers
requires normalization.

### M4.9 Spectrograms show absolute frequencies [LOW]
Tested in: HW3 Q16.

Absolute values are present, but the *information* is in *patterns* (band
shapes, formant transitions, contours). Two spectrograms of "ba" by different
speakers will have different absolute formants but the same general shape.

## Domain 5: Phonemes & Phonemic Principle

### M5.1 [i]/[ɪ] are allophones in English [CRITICAL]
Tested in: HW4 Q27 (Lucille's -4); cross-linguistic phonemic analysis questions.

FALSE. They are **separate phonemes** in English. Minimal pairs: *bit*/*beat*,
*sit*/*seat*, *fit*/*feet*. They're allophones in some other languages
(Spanish has only /i/), but in English they contrast.

**This is the highest-priority misconception for HW4.** Students project
English categorizations onto other languages and vice versa.

**Repair move:** ask the student to find a minimal pair distinguishing [i]
and [ɪ] in English. They will. Then prompt: "What does that minimal pair
tell us about phonemic status?"

### M5.2 Phonemes are "real" sounds and allophones are weird variations [HIGH]
Tested in: Day 11 slides.

INVERTED. **Allophones are physically real** (the actual sounds spoken/heard).
**Phonemes are mental abstractions** that group allophones together.

### M5.3 Same IPA in two languages = same phoneme [HIGH]
Tested in: HW4 (cross-linguistic phonemic analysis).

Phonemes are language-specific categorizations. [p] and [pʰ] are the same
phoneme in English (both /p/) but different phonemes in Hindi.

### M5.4 If two sounds appear in similar contexts, they're allophones [HIGH]
Tested in: HW4 Q11-Q14.

INVERTED. Similar contexts → overlapping → **separate phonemes**. The
diagnostic for allophones is *complementary* distribution (NO contextual
overlap), plus phonetic similarity.

### M5.5 Complementary distribution means the same word can have either sound [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW4 distributional questions.

Complementary distribution means the sounds occur in *different* contexts.
Swapping yields a non-word or unusual pronunciation, not a different word.

### M5.6 Phonetic similarity isn't important — only distribution matters [HIGH]
Tested in: HW4; Day 11 slides on [h]/[ŋ].

Similarity is part of the phonemic principle. English [h] and [ŋ] are in
complementary distribution but are NOT allophones — they fail the similarity
test.

### M5.7 Minimal pairs are required for phonemic status [HIGH]
Tested in: HW4 Q12-Q15; Hindi [b]/[b̤] case (Midterm 2 review B1 KEY).

Minimal pairs are *clear evidence* of overlapping distribution. But
overlapping is the actual diagnostic. Hindi [b]/[b̤] are phonemes shown by
overlapping environments (#__a, #__i), not strict minimal pairs.

### M5.8 The underlying form is the way the word is spelled [HIGH]
Tested in: HW4 throughout.

Spelling has nothing to do with the UR. The UR is determined by distributional
analysis, not orthography.

### M5.9 Whichever allophone appears most often is the UR [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW4 Q26; HW5 Q10.

Not always. The UR is the one with the **broadest distribution** (the elsewhere
case). Usually correlates with frequency but not always. The diagnostic is
which choice yields the simplest rules.

### M5.10 A phoneme is a sound that never appears in a minimal pair [HIGH]
Tested in: Quiz 4 Q5.

FALSE. A phoneme is **a *lexically contrastive* sound**. Minimal pairs are
evidence FOR phonemic status, not against it.

### M5.11 Free variation is the same as allophony [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Midterm 2 study guide explicitly names free variation.

**Different categories.** Allophones are positionally restricted (predictable).
Free variants are interchangeable in the same context. Example: word-final [t]
in English can be released [t] or unreleased [t˺] — same context, both
acceptable, free variation.

## Domain 6: Phonological Rules & Notation

### M6.1 Phonological rules describe physical articulations [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW4 rule writing.

Rules are *abstract symbolic mappings* between underlying and surface
representations. They look like physical events ("nasalization") but operate
on symbolic representations.

### M6.2 Slashes and brackets are interchangeable [HIGH]
Tested in: HW4 throughout; HW5.

They're NOT.
- `/X/` = phoneme / underlying form
- `[X]` = phonetic / surface form

Mixing them in a single rule is a notational error signaling level-of-
representation confusion.

### M6.3 You can list any group of sounds as a "natural class" [CRITICAL]
Tested in: HW4 Q6; HW5 Q11.

A natural class must (1) share a feature, AND (2) **exhaust the sounds in the
language with that feature**. [p l w] is not a class — many other consonants
share their commonalities.

### M6.4 Wider natural class is always better [HIGH]
Tested in: HW5 Q11 (Lucille missed).

INVERTED. The **narrowest exhaustive class** is the right answer. For Maltese
{t, d, s, n}, "sonorants" or "voiced consonants" are too broad — "coronals"
is the narrowest exhaustive class.

**Repair move:** ask the student "are there sounds in the language that share
that feature but aren't in your data? If yes, your class is too broad."

### M6.5 Listing sounds where features should be used [HIGH]
Tested in: HW4 rule writing.

A student who writes `/[b/d/g]/ → [β/ð/ɣ] / V_V` is missing the natural-class
generalization. Correct: `/[+voiced stop]/ → [+fricative] / V_V`.

### M6.6 All processes are assimilation [HIGH]
Tested in: HW4; HW5 Q12 (Lucille missed).

**Five distinct families:** assimilation, dissimilation, fortition/lenition,
epenthesis/deletion, metathesis. Knowing the type matters for rule formulation.

### M6.7 Rule application is the same as rule reading [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Midterm 2 review C2-C3.

Rule reading: "/p/ → [b] / V_V" reads as "/p/ becomes [b] in the V_V environment."
Rule application: given UR /apa/ and this rule, derive [aba]. Application is
**mechanical** — given UR and rule, surface form is fully determined.

### M6.8 Epenthesis adds a sound randomly [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Midterm 2 review C4 (Farsi).

Epenthesis is **constrained** by phonotactics. A vowel inserted between two
consonants is epenthesized to *repair* an illegal cluster, not arbitrarily.

### M6.9 Two competing rule-set analyses are equally valid [HIGH]
Tested in: HW5 Q9-Q10 (Lucille missed); Midterm 2 review C4.

Sometimes both technically work, but you choose by **simplicity** (Occam's
razor). Maltese problem and Farsi pluralization both test this directly.

## Domain 7: Allomorphs & Morpho-Phonology

### M7.1 Allophones and allomorphs are the same thing [HIGH]
Tested in: Day 13 slides; HW4.

Different units:
- Allophones = variants of a *phoneme* (a single sound category). Example:
  [pʰ] and [p] are allophones of /p/.
- Allomorphs = variants of a *morpheme* (a meaningful unit). Example:
  [-s], [-z], [-əz] are allomorphs of the English plural.

### M7.2 The shorter allomorph is always the UR [HIGH]
Tested in: HW5 Q10.

No. UR is by **broadest distribution**, not shortest form. English plural [-z]
is the UR even though [-s] is also short. Negative prefix /ɪn-/ is the UR
even though [ɪl-], [ɪɹ-], [ɪm-] are all the same length.

### M7.3 Every alternation is voicing assimilation [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW4 process classification.

Voicing assimilation is one common process. Alternations can also be place
assimilation (negative prefix), epenthesis (English -əz), deletion (English
t/d-deletion), or metathesis.

### M7.4 Loanword phonology has different rules [LOW]
Tested in: Day 12 slides.

Same processes, different triggers. Loanwords expose constraints that weren't
visible before in the receiving language.

### M7.5 Stem-and-affix sequences pronounce as their literal concatenation [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW4 alternation questions.

Often they do, but when violating phonotactics, the language repairs the form.
*dog* + *s* = *[dɔg-z]* (not *[dɔg-s]*) because *[gs]* would violate voicing
co-occurrence.

### M7.6 Allomorph selection is unrelated to syllable structure [HIGH]
Tested in: HW5 Q4, Q9, Q10.

Often is — the [-əz] plural exists *to repair* an illegal sibilant cluster.
Phonotactic constraints drive allomorph selection. Maltese: [il-]/[l-]
distribution depends on whether the stem syllable has an onset.

## Domain 8: Syllable Structure & Sonority

### M8.1 Syllable count = letter count [HIGH]
Tested in: HW5 Q16.

It's *vowel-sound count*. *Bake* has one syllable [beɪk], not two.

### M8.2 Diphthongs are two syllables [HIGH]
Tested in: HW5 Q16.

Diphthongs occupy a single nucleus. *Bite* [baɪt] is one syllable.

### M8.3 Onsets and codas are required [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW5 Q1.

In English, only the nucleus is required. *Egg* [ɛg] has no onset; *pie* [paɪ]
has no coda. Some languages do require an onset (or disallow codas), but English
doesn't.

### M8.4 Maximal Onset Principle = put all consonants in the onset [HIGH]
Tested in: HW5 Q16.

MOP is **constrained by language-specific phonotactics**. *Empty* is not
[ɛ.mpti] because [mpt] isn't a legal English onset. MOP says "as many as
legally allowed," not "all of them."

### M8.5 Sonority Sequencing applies to all clusters [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW5 Q1; Day 14 slides.

SSG is a strong cross-linguistic tendency, but languages have exceptions —
especially with /s/ + obstruent in onsets (English *spy*, *star*, *ski* are
SSG-violating but legal).

### M8.6 All codas are equal in weight [HIGH]
Tested in: HW5 Q17-Q18.

In English, only *stressed* syllables get a mora from their coda consonants.
*Raptor* ['ræp.təɹ] — first-syllable [p] adds a mora; second-syllable [ɹ]
doesn't.

### M8.7 Mora assignment is the same across languages [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW5 Q17-Q18 (Greek).

Languages vary: Latin (V and coda C both get moras), Japanese (every C and V
gets a mora), English (coda only in stressed syllables).

### M8.8 Cross-linguistic borrowing preserves the original syllable structure [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Day 14 slides; Midterm 2 review D2.

It doesn't. Borrowed words are *repaired* to fit the receiving language's
constraints. *Christmas* → Hawaiian *kelikimaka*.

### M8.9 Light/dark /l/ is a free-variant choice [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Quiz 5 Q2; Day 14 slides.

It's *positional* — light /l/ in onsets, dark [ɫ] in codas (in General American).

### M8.10 Sonority and voicing are the same thing [HIGH]
Tested in: HW5 Q15-Q16; Quiz 5 Q3.

Voicing is a binary feature; sonority is a hierarchy. Voiced fricatives are
MORE sonorant than voiceless ones, but LESS sonorant than nasals (which are
also voiced). Cannot predict sonority from voicing alone.

### M8.11 Speech errors can swap any two segments [MEDIUM]
Tested in: HW5 Q15; Quiz 5 Q5.

Constrained by syllable position. Onsets swap with onsets, nuclei with nuclei,
codas with codas. The "skit in the dum" error (swap of coda and onset across
words) is unlikely.

### M8.12 All languages allow CCC onsets [LOW]
Tested in: Day 14 cross-linguistic.

CV is the universal. Onset/coda complexity varies widely.

## Domain 9: Distributional Analysis Methodology

### M9.1 The data tells you the answer immediately [HIGH]
Tested in: HW4 throughout.

No — distributional analysis is *iterative*. List the contexts, look for
patterns, refine the rule. Students who try to leap to a conclusion miss
patterns.

### M9.2 Skipping the distributional analysis and jumping to a conclusion [HIGH]
Tested in: HW4 throughout.

Common when students think they "see" a pattern. The discipline of writing
out *every* environment is what catches the analysis.

### M9.3 Confusing complementary and overlapping distribution [HIGH]
Tested in: HW4; Quiz 4.

**Complementary** = NEVER the same context.
**Overlapping** = at least one shared context.

Students often think "they appear in different positions sometimes" means
complementary. That's incorrect — even one shared context = overlapping.

### M9.4 Picking the wrong UR [HIGH]
Tested in: HW4 Q26; HW5 Q10.

Students often pick the form they see most in the data, or the "default"
English form, rather than the form yielding simplest derivation. Walk through
both candidates, count rules required.

### M9.5 Treating each language's analysis independently [LOW]
Tested in: Cross-linguistic patterns.

If voicing-between-vowels is the rule in Cree, Spanish, and Old English,
that's a cross-linguistic pattern worth noting. Helps with rule confidence.

## Domain 10: Praat & Measurement (Practica)

### M10.1 The Praat formant tracker is reliable [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Practicum 1.

It's not. The tracker can mislabel, especially in noisy regions or at vowel
transitions. Students need to verify by visual inspection of the spectrogram.

### M10.2 Vowel formant values are absolute [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Practicum 1.

Different speakers will have different absolute formants for "the same" vowel.

### M10.3 Take formant measurements at vowel edges [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Practicum 1.

NO — take at the vowel midpoint. Edges have coarticulation effects from
adjacent consonants.

### M10.4 Spectral slice = spectrogram [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Practicum 1.

A spectral slice is amplitude vs frequency at one moment (snapshot). A
spectrogram is amplitude (darkness) vs frequency (y) over time (x).

### M10.5 Categorical perception is acoustic [LOW]
Tested in: Practicum 2 (skunk → kunk experiment).

Categorical perception is *perceptual*. The acoustic [k] in *skunk* and the
[k] in *kunk* (with [s] removed) are identical, but listeners perceive them
differently because of context.

### M10.6 Word boundaries in fluent speech are acoustic [MEDIUM]
Tested in: Practicum 2.

They're perceptual. Listeners infer word boundaries; there's often no
acoustic gap between words.

### M10.7 Sonorants are easy to segment [LOW]
Tested in: Practicum 2.

In fluent speech, sonorants (approximants, nasals) blur into adjacent vowels.
Sibilants and stop releases give clear acoustic edges.

## How the catalog is used

When `repair_misconceptions` fires, the orchestrator can match a student's
utterance to entries in this catalog. The repair move (where given) is the
preferred response — it scaffolds discovery rather than naming the answer.

When `validate_pre_knowledge` or `diagnose_prerequisite_gaps` fires, the
orchestrator can probe for these misconceptions specifically — the catalog
tells the orchestrator what to look for.

When designing new skills, this catalog is the source for example
misconceptions. Don't reinvent — look here first.

## Maintenance

When new misconceptions are observed in student sessions, add them to the
appropriate domain section using this template:

```
### M[domain].[number] Short title [PRIORITY]
Tested in: [where it appears]

[1-3 sentence description of the misconception]

**Repair move:** [optional — the Socratic move that addresses it without
naming the correct answer]
```

Maintain priority levels:
- CRITICAL — repeatedly observed, high cost
