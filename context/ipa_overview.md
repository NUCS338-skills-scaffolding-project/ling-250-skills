# The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)

Reference for what the IPA is, why Ling 250 uses it, and how to read it.
Used by skills that need to remind students why phonetic transcription
matters and how it differs from ordinary spelling.

---

## What it is

The IPA is a standardized system of symbols for representing speech sounds.
It was developed by the International Phonetic Association in 1886 and is
used worldwide by linguists, language teachers, speech pathologists, and
dictionary editors.

Think of it as a periodic table for phonetics: one symbol per distinct
sound, consistent across every language.

## The guiding principle: one sound, one symbol

Every distinct speech sound gets its own symbol. The same symbol always
represents the same sound, regardless of which language it appears in.

- [p] in English *pet* is the same [p] in Spanish *perro* or Japanese *pan*.
- [ʃ] is the "sh" sound whether it's spelled *sh* (English *ship*),
  *sch* (German *Schule*), *ch* (French *chat*), or *x* (Portuguese *xadrez*).

This is the opposite of how alphabetic spelling works in most languages,
where the relationship between letters and sounds is often inconsistent.

## Why we don't use English spelling

English orthography evolved over centuries, borrowed from multiple
languages, and froze in place before pronunciation finished changing.
The result is that English spelling is a poor guide to pronunciation:

- **Same letter, different sounds:** the *c* in *cat* [k] vs. *cell* [s]
- **Different letters, same sound:** *ship* [ʃ], *nation* [ʃ], *special* [ʃ],
  *sugar* [ʃ], *ocean* [ʃ]
- **Silent letters:** *knight*, *psalm*, *comb*
- **Letters that vary by dialect:** rhotic *r* in American vs. non-rhotic
  British English

If you want to describe what a sound actually *is* — not how it happens to
be spelled in one particular language — you need a system that breaks free
of any one language's spelling conventions. That's what IPA gives you.

## What IPA symbols look like

Some IPA symbols look just like familiar letters and mean roughly what
you'd expect: [p], [t], [k], [b], [d], [g], [f], [s], [m], [n], [l].

Others look unfamiliar because English spelling doesn't have a single
letter for them:

| Symbol | As in       | Why it looks weird                                |
|--------|-------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| [θ]    | *thing*     | English spells it *th*, but it's one sound       |
| [ð]    | *this*      | English spells it *th* again — different sound   |
| [ʃ]    | *ship*      | English spells it *sh*                           |
| [ʒ]    | *measure*   | English has no consistent spelling for this      |
| [tʃ]   | *chip*      | English spells it *ch*                           |
| [dʒ]   | *jug*       | English spells it *j* or *g* or *dge*            |
| [ŋ]    | *sing*      | English spells it *ng*                           |
| [ɹ]    | *red*       | English uses *r* (which means different things   |
|        |             | in other languages)                              |
| [ʔ]    | *uh-oh*     | English doesn't spell this sound at all          |
| [ɾ]    | *butter*    | The "flap" in American English — also unspelled  |
| [j]    | *yes*       | Looks like a consonantal *y*                     |

## Diacritics

Diacritics are small marks added to symbols to indicate finer-grained
detail — aspiration, nasalization, voicing changes, length, etc. For most
of Ling 250, the plain symbols are enough; diacritics come up in more
detailed phonetic transcription.

## The bottom line

**Forget English spelling when you transcribe.** What matters is the
sound a speaker actually produces, not the letter a dictionary shows.
If you find yourself writing down letters because that's how the word
is spelled, stop and ask yourself what sounds you're actually making.
