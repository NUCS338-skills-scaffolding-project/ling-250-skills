# HW3: Waveforms, Spectra, and Spectrograms

## Unit / Days Covered

Days 6-7 of the course; Zsiga Ch. 4-5.

Day 5 acoustics fundamentals (period, frequency, amplitude, F0,
harmonics, formants, source-filter) and Days 1-3 consonant articulation
are prerequisite and may be probed by `validate-prereqs`.

## Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Distinguish source (vocal folds) from filter (vocal tract)
- Explain the source-filter independence principle: same vowel at
  different F0 = same formants, different harmonic spacing
- Read F0 from harmonic spacing on a spectrum
- Identify F1, F2, F3 from formant peaks (count up from bottom)
- Map F1 to vowel height (high vowel = LOW F1 — inverse) and F2 to
  vowel backness (front vowel = high F2)
- Read a spectrogram: x = time, y = frequency, darkness = amplitude
- Identify on a spectrogram: vowels (formant bands), stops (closure
  + burst + aspiration), fricatives (high-frequency noise),
  sonorants (lower-amplitude formants), nasals (sudden intensity
  change + nasal formants)
- Read formant transitions to identify consonant place of articulation
  (labial = formants point down; alveolar = F2 → 1700 Hz; velar =
  F2-F3 pinch)
- Mark segment boundaries on a waveform
- Calculate harmonics (Nth harmonic = N × F0) and predict harmonic
  spacing from F0
- Recognize the VOT distinctions: long-lag (aspirated voiceless),
  short-lag (zero-VOT voiced), negative (prevoiced) — and their
  contextual distribution in English

## Concepts Required

### Building on Days 1-5 (prerequisite)

- Vocal tract anatomy and articulation features (place, manner,
  voicing)
- Wave terminology: period, frequency, amplitude, periodic vs aperiodic
- F0 as fundamental frequency, harmonics as integer multiples
- Formants as filter resonances of the vocal tract
- Source-filter theory introduction

### Day 6 — Waveforms in detail + intro to spectrograms

- Periodic vs aperiodic mapped to voicing (voiced = periodic;
  voiceless = aperiodic)
- The three-phase anatomy of a stop consonant in the waveform:
  closure, release burst, aspiration (for voiceless aspirated stops)
- Voice Onset Time (VOT) — the interval between stop release and
  voicing of the next segment
- Negative VOT (prevoicing), short-lag (~10-30 ms, unaspirated),
  long-lag (~30-100 ms, aspirated)
- English /p t k/ are typically long-lag in word-initial position;
  /b d g/ are typically short-lag in phrase-initial; prevoiced
  intervocalically
- Fricative waveforms: voiceless = pure aperiodic noise; voiced =
  periodic + noise components

### Day 7 — Spectrograms

- Spectrogram dimensions: x = time, y = frequency, darkness =
  amplitude (intensity)
- Wide-band vs narrow-band spectrograms (default is wide-band,
  emphasizes formants)
- Vowel signatures: dark horizontal bands = formants
- F1 ↔ vowel height (high vowel = LOW F1)
- F2 ↔ vowel backness (front vowel = high F2)
- Stop signatures: closure (silent gap), release burst (vertical
  spike), aspiration (aperiodic noise band)
- Fricative signatures: random noise distribution; energy by place
  ([s, z] concentrated at 5000-6000 Hz; [ʃ, ʒ] at 2500 Hz; [f, θ, v,
  ð] diffuse; [h] tracks adjacent vowel formants)
- Sonorant signatures: vowel-like formants but lower amplitude
  ([l] = abrupt boundary, low F2; [ɹ] = very low F3 diagnostic;
  [w], [j] like [u], [i])
- Nasal signatures: abrupt amplitude drop; nasal formants ~200 Hz
  and ~2000 Hz; place read from adjacent vowel transitions
- Formant transitions for stop POA: labial = all formant edges
  point DOWN; alveolar = F2 → ~1700 Hz; velar = F2-F3 pinch
- Vowel formant variability across speakers, languages, sexes,
  and contexts — IPA labels phonemic categories, not exact
  acoustic realizations

### Source-filter independence (Day 5 → Day 7 bridge)

- F0 affects only harmonic spacing, not formant frequencies
- Same vowel at low F0 = closely-spaced harmonics + formant pattern
- Same vowel at high F0 = widely-spaced harmonics + same formant pattern
- Formula: Nth harmonic = N × F0; doubled F0 = doubled harmonic
  spacing

## Question Types

- **F0 and formant reasoning** (Q1-Q3, 3 pts) — Reasoning about
  what changes with pitch vs vowel quality. Tests source-filter
  independence directly.

- **Spectrum/spectrogram terminology** (Q4-Q6, 3 pts) — What does
  F0 affect? What do tongue position changes affect? What does
  darkness represent on a spectrogram?

- **Reading "cup" vowel spectra (male vs female)** (Q7-Q9, 3 pts)
  — Two spectral slices of [ʌ] in *cup*. Questions probe specific
  values (which formant has amplitude ~50 dB?), F0 differences
  between the two speakers, and which speaker is female.

- **Three vowel spectra (A, B, C)** (Q10-Q15, 6 pts) — Reading
  specific values off the three spectra. Identifying which vowel
  each represents based on F1/F2 patterns. Calculating harmonic
  values (Nth harmonic = N × F0).

- **Spectrogram-to-phrase matching** (Q16, 6 pts) — Three
  spectrograms (A, B, C), three target phrases. Students must
  identify which spectrogram matches which phrase by reading
  consonant signatures (number of words, syllable count, vowel
  durations, fricatives, stops, nasals).

- **IPA transcription of a sentence** (Q17, 5 pts) — "blue cats
  went to school" → [ˈblu] [ˈkæts] [ˈwɛnt] [tə] [ˈskul]. Tests
  stress marks and the unstressed-schwa pattern (*to* → [tə]).

- **Waveform segment-boundary identification** (Q18, 14 pts) —
  14 sub-questions, each asking which arrow on a waveform marks
  the edge of a specific segment in words "dose," "mood,"
  "cheat," "mess." Tests recognizing voicing, sibilant noise,
  vowel onset, closure gaps.

## Common Misconceptions

Course-wide misconceptions especially relevant to HW3:

- **F0 and F1 are the same thing** — F0 is the fundamental
  frequency (rate of vocal fold vibration; perceived as pitch).
  F1 is the lowest formant (lowest amplified resonance). Different
  physical quantities. **Single most-confused pair in vowel
  acoustics. High-priority entry.**
- **Harmonics and formants are the same** — harmonics are
  multiples of F0 (source). Formants are amplified harmonics
  (filter). Every vowel has the same harmonics given fixed F0;
  what differs is which harmonics are formant peaks.
- **Higher F0 → narrower harmonic spacing** — INVERTED. **Higher
  F0 → wider harmonic spacing.** Harmonics at integer multiples
  of F0; doubling F0 doubles the spacing. Tested on HW3 Q2.
- **F1 corresponds to vowel backness** — INVERTED. F1 ↔ height,
  F2 ↔ backness. Mnemonic mismatch is the single most-common
  formant-reading error.
- **High vowels have HIGH F1** — INVERTED. **High vowels have LOW
  F1.** Low vowels have high F1. Inverse relationship: open jaw
  shrinks pharynx, raising first resonance.
- **Darkness on a spectrogram is frequency** — Darkness is
  *amplitude* (intensity at that frequency, at that time).
  Frequency is on the y-axis.
- **Place of articulation can be read directly from the consonant
  on a spectrogram** — For nasals and stops, place is mostly read
  from the *adjacent vowel's* formant transitions. Counterintuitive.
- **All speakers have the same formant values for the same vowel**
  — Different speakers produce different absolute formants because
  of vocal tract length differences. Comparing across speakers
  requires normalization.
- **F0 changes affect formants** — They don't. F0 is source; formants
  are filter. Independent.
- **VOT is the same as voicing** — VOT is a *timing* measure, not
  a binary feature. Continuous variable.
- **All English voiced stops are prevoiced** — They're not. English
  /b d g/ in word-initial / phrase-initial position have short-lag
  (positive but small) VOT, NOT negative. Prevoicing reliable
  intervocalically. **Catalog refinement from Quiz 3 Q1.**
- **Aspiration is part of the consonant** — Aspiration is the
  *delay in voicing onset* after stop release, not an extra "puff"
  attached to the consonant.
- **Voiceless fricatives have no waveform** — They have a clear
  waveform; it's just *aperiodic*.

Assignment-specific patterns to watch for:

- **F0 vs F1 confusion in spectrum reading (Q7-Q8)** — When asked
  about F0, students sometimes look at F1 (or vice versa). The
  diagnostic: F0 is the rate of vocal fold vibration → harmonic
  spacing on a spectrum. F1 is the lowest formant peak. Different
  physical quantities, different visual cues.
- **Misreading harmonic spacing as a formant property (Q2)** —
  Harmonics depend ONLY on F0. Formants are independent of F0.
- **Reading spectrograms by counting words rather than acoustic
  features (Q16)** — Students who don't know consonant signatures
  default to "this spectrogram looks like 4 words." The skill must
  coach the student to look at *features* not *gross shape*.
- **Marking segment boundaries by visual symmetry (Q18)** —
  Wrong answers come from picking the arrow at the "obvious"
  amplitude change without considering whether that's
  consonant-to-vowel, vowel-to-consonant, or within-segment.
- **Cross-speaker comparisons without normalization (Q9)** —
  Students who treat formants as fixed across speakers will
  struggle.

## What Success Looks Like

A correct student:

- Distinguishes F0 from F1 by recognizing that F0 = rate of vocal
  fold vibration = pitch, while F1 = lowest formant = first
  amplified resonance
- Reads harmonic spacing on a spectrum to estimate F0 (smaller
  spacing → lower F0)
- Counts formants from the bottom up: F1 first, then F2, F3, ...
- Maps F1 to height (low F1 = high vowel) and F2 to backness
  (high F2 = front vowel) using the inverse-for-F1 rule
- For spectrograms, identifies segment categories first (vowel,
  stop, fricative, nasal, sonorant) before trying to identify
  specific sounds
- Reads formant transitions to determine consonant place of
  articulation (labial = formants drop; alveolar = F2 ~ 1700 Hz;
  velar = F2-F3 pinch)
- Recognizes that male/female speakers produce different absolute
  formants for the same vowel; compares within-speaker rather
  than across
- For waveform segment-boundary questions, asks "what kind of
  segment am I leaving? what kind am I entering? what visual
  change marks that?"
- Calculates harmonic values mechanically (5th harmonic of F0=110
  → 5 × 110 = 550 Hz)

A student on the right track but not yet there typically:

- Gets formants from the spectrogram but mixes up F1 vs F2 mapping
- Reads harmonic spacing correctly but inverts the F0 → spacing
  direction
- Identifies broad categories on spectrograms but can't connect
  to specific phrases (Q16 territory)
- Marks segment boundaries by amplitude rather than by the type
  of segment being entered/left

## What the Tutor Should NOT Do

- **Do not read formants off the spectrogram for the student.**
  Walk them through identifying peaks themselves.
- **Do not identify a vowel from formants before the student has
  walked through F1 and F2 individually** and applied the
  inverse-F1 rule for height.
- **Do not reveal which spectrogram matches which phrase (Q16)**
  before the student has identified the consonant signatures
  in each.
- **Do not explain Praat measurement procedure** in detail —
  that's the practicum.
- **Do not state formant values as exact** — always discuss them
  as relative ranges that vary by speaker.
- **Do not name the segment boundary on Q18 before the student
  has classified the segments on either side.**
- **Do not transcribe Q17 sentence for the student.** Walk through
  it word by word, asking about stress and unstressed-syllable
  vowels.
- **Do not assume the student knows which formant changes with
  vowel height vs backness** — many students inherit the wrong
  mnemonic.
