# HW5: Syllable Structure

## Unit / Days Covered

Days 14-15 (first half) of the course. Day 14 covers the syllable
structure content. Day 15 first half is recap; second half (speech
perception, categorical perception, identification/discrimination
tests) is **post-HW5** and not relevant.

Days 8-13 phonemic analysis methodology is heavily prerequisite —
the Maltese dataset in Parts A-C of this assignment is a phonemic-
analysis-style problem applied to syllable-driven alternations.

## Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Define syllable structure constituents: onset, nucleus, coda,
  rhyme (= nucleus + coda)
- Count syllables in English words (= count vowel sounds, not
  vowel letters)
- Apply Maximal Onset Principle (MOP) subject to language-specific
  phonotactic constraints
- Order sounds along the sonority hierarchy (vowels > glides >
  liquids > nasals > voiced fricatives > voiceless fricatives >
  voiced stops > voiceless stops)
- Identify syllables that conform to vs violate the Sonority
  Sequencing Generalization (SSG)
- Analyze cross-linguistic variation in syllable structure (CV
  universal; ranges from Hawaiian C-only to English CCCVCCCC)
- Predict phonotactic repair in borrowed words (epenthesis,
  deletion, segmental adaptation)
- Apply mora assignment rules: light = 1 mora; heavy = 2 moras
  (CV: or CVC); superheavy = 3+
- Recognize that English coda consonants only contribute moras in
  stressed syllables
- Identify natural classes in conditioning environments for rules
- Conduct distributional analysis on a syllable-driven alternation
  (the Maltese definite-prefix problem)
- Choose UR by simplicity for an alternation with two candidate URs
- Predict speech-error patterns from syllable position (onsets swap
  with onsets, etc.)

### Day 14 — Syllable Structure

- Syllable as a grouping unit; native speaker intuitions
- Counting syllables = counting vowel sounds (not letters)
- Syllable structure tree: σ → Onset + Rhyme; Rhyme → Nucleus + Coda
- Onset, nucleus, coda definitions
- Why "Rhyme" — from poetics; rhyme = the unit of rhyming
- English syllable templates:
  - Onset: ∅, C, CC, or CCC
  - Nucleus: V, V: (long), or VV (diphthong)
  - Coda: ∅, C, CC, or CC + suffix
- Onset and coda optional in English; nucleus required
- Maximal Onset Principle (MOP): put as many consonants as possible
  in onset, subject to language-specific constraints
- Sonority hierarchy (8 ranks)
- Sonority Sequencing Generalization (SSG): nucleus is sonority peak;
  sonority decreases outward from nucleus
- Conforming vs violating syllables (English /s/ + obstruent is
  the canonical SSG violation that's still legal)
- Syllabification algorithm: project nucleus → project rhyme →
  project syllable → onset formation (MOP) → coda formation
- Cross-linguistic variation:
  - Universal: every language allows CV
  - Hawaiian: (C)V(:), no codas
  - Japanese: V, CV, CVN
  - Finnish: CV mostly
  - English: complex (up to CCCVCCCC)
- Borrowed-word phonotactic repair: segment mapping then structural
  repair (epenthesis or deletion)
- Long vowels and the nucleus (occupy two positions)
- Mora (μ) — unit of syllable weight
- Light syllable (1 mora = CV); heavy (2 moras = CV: or CVC); superheavy (3+)
- English: coda C contributes mora only in stressed syllables
- Mora rules vary by language (Latin, Japanese, English, etc.)
- Syllable-position-conditioned rules: English clear/dark /l/
  ([l] in onset, [ɫ] in coda); flapping (/t,d/ → [ɾ] between
  stressed and unstressed syllables)
- Speech-error evidence for syllables: spoonerisms preserve
  syllable position
- Language-game evidence (French verlan)

### Cross-cutting

- Implicational typological universal: if a language allows codas,
  it also allows syllables without codas
- Phonotactic constraints drive allomorph selection (the bridge
  between HW4 and HW5)

## Common Misconceptions

Course-wide misconceptions especially relevant to HW5:

### Syllable structure-related

- **Syllable count = letter count.** It's *vowel-sound count*. *Bake*
  has one syllable [beɪk], not two (despite "a" and "e" letters).
- **Diphthongs are two syllables.** Diphthongs occupy a single
  nucleus. *Bite* [baɪt] is one syllable.
- **Onsets and codas are required.** In English, only the nucleus
  is required. *Egg* [ɛg] has no onset; *pie* [paɪ] has no coda.
- **Maximal Onset Principle = put all consonants in the onset.**
  MOP is constrained by language-specific phonotactics. *Empty*
  is not [ɛ.mpti] because [mpt] isn't a legal English onset.
- **Sonority Sequencing applies to all clusters.** SSG is a
  cross-linguistic tendency, but languages have exceptions —
  especially with /s/ at the start of onset clusters (English
  *spy*, *star*, *ski* are SSG-violating but legal).
- **All codas are equal in weight.** In English, only *stressed*
  syllables get a mora from their coda consonants. *Raptor*
  ['ræp.təɹ] — first-syllable [p] adds a mora; second-syllable
  [ɹ] doesn't.
- **Mora assignment is the same across languages.** Languages
  vary: Latin, Japanese, English all have different mora rules.
- **Cross-linguistic borrowing preserves the original syllable
  structure.** It doesn't. Words are *repaired* to fit the
  receiving language's constraints.
- **Light/dark /l/ is a free-variant choice.** It's *positional*
  — light /l/ in onsets, dark [ɫ] in codas. Quiz 5 Q2 confirms.
- **Sonority and voicing are the same thing.** Voicing is binary;
  sonority is a hierarchy. Voiced fricatives are MORE sonorant
  than voiceless ones, but LESS sonorant than nasals.
- **All languages allow CCC onsets.** False. CV is the universal.
  Onset/coda complexity varies widely.
- **Implicational universal — onsets and codas are equally
  distributed.** False. **If a language allows codas, it also
  allows syllables without codas.** Quiz 5 Q1.
- **Speech errors can swap any two segments.** Constrained by
  syllable position. Onsets swap with onsets, nuclei with nuclei,
  codas with codas. Quiz 5 Q5 + HW5 Q15.

### Allomorph / UR (carrying forward from HW4)

- **Shorter allomorphs are always the UR.** No — broadest
  distribution matters more.
- **Allomorph selection is unrelated to syllable structure.**
  Often is — the [-əz] plural exists *to repair* an illegal
  sibilant cluster. Phonotactic constraints drive allomorph
  selection.
- **Wider natural class is always better.** No — Q11 tests this.
  The narrowest exhaustive class is the right answer.

Assignment-specific patterns to watch for (the Maltese problem
is the conceptual heart of HW5):

- **Q3 (initial vs final sound).** Likely error: choose "final
  sound of the noun stem" or "vowel type." The diagnostic is
  the *initial* sound (which determines syllabification with
  the prefix).
- **Q4 (no-onset vs onset).** Conceptual key: [l-] before
  vowel-initial (no-onset) stems; [il-] before consonant-initial
  (onset) stems. Easy to miss if thinking in terms of consonants
  vs vowels rather than syllable shape.
- **Q9-Q10 (UR motivation).** "Which analysis is better?" The
  right reasoning: /l-/ as UR plus epenthesis is simpler than
  /il-/ as UR plus deletion.
- **Q11 (coronals).** Picking "voiced consonant" or "sonorants"
  — both include the targets but are too broad. **Coronals** is
  the narrowest exhaustive class for /t, d, s, n/.
- **Q12 (assimilation).** Picking "epenthesis" because of the
  [il-] form. The diagnostic: the [l] becomes identical to the
  following stem-initial consonant — that's textbook assimilation.

## What Success Looks Like

A correct student:

- Counts syllables by counting vowel sounds, not letters
- Applies MOP subject to phonotactic constraints (knows that
  not all clusters are legal as onsets)
- Walks through the sonority hierarchy when analyzing syllable
  shapes
- For SSP-conformance, identifies the sonority peak first, then
  checks decrease outward
- Recognizes English /s/+obstruent as the canonical SSG-violating
  but legal cluster
- For the Maltese problem, lists environments for each allomorph
  systematically, identifies that the conditioning is the *initial
  sound of the stem*, recognizes the syllable-structural framing
  (no-onset vs onset stem), and picks the simpler UR (/l-/ +
  epenthesis)
- For Q11, identifies the narrowest exhaustive natural class
  (coronals, not "sonorants" or "voiced")
- For mora questions, applies the language-specific rules
  (English: coda mora only in stressed syllables)
- For speech errors, identifies which syllable position is being
  swapped (onset, nucleus, or coda) and predicts the surface
  form by maintaining position

A student on the right track but not yet there typically:

- Identifies allomorph variants and contexts but picks the wrong
  UR (the longer one)
- Identifies a natural class but picks one that's too broad
- Counts syllables right but misapplies MOP
- Knows the sonority hierarchy but doesn't apply SSG to specific
  syllables

## What the Tutor Should NOT Do

- **Do not syllabify a word for the student.** Walk them through
  identifying nuclei, then onsets, then codas.
- **Do not tell the student which Cs go where.** Walk them through
  MOP and language constraints.
- **Do not reveal mora count** before they've identified syllable
  weight category and applied the language's mora rules.
- **Do not classify a syllable as SSG-violating** before the
  student has drawn the sonority contour.
- **Do not pick the UR for them in allomorph questions** — walk
  them through the simplicity comparison.
- **Do not describe phonotactic repair as a single rule** — guide
  them through the two-phase process (segment mapping, then
  structural repair).
- **Do not name the natural class** for Q11 before the student
  has listed the shared features. **Especially**: don't accept a
  too-broad answer — walk them through "is there any sound that
  shares those features but isn't in the data?"
- **Do not name the process type** (assimilation, epenthesis, etc.)
  for the Maltese problem before the student has described what's
  changing.

## Skills Relevant to This Assignment

HW5 uses the Round 2 skills more concentrated than HW4 — the
Maltese dataset is one extended problem.

- **`analyze-dist`** (Round 2) — for Q1-Q4 (collecting environments
  of [il-] vs [l-]). Same flow as HW4.
- **`probe-min-pair`** (Round 2) — minor use; minimal pairs aren't
  central evidence type in HW5.
- **`write-phon-rule`** (Round 2) — for Q6 and Q8 (dual UR rule
  formulations). Should specifically scaffold the
  /UR/ → [allomorph] / __ [environment] notation with environment
  terms drawn from a controlled vocabulary (onset, no-onset, coda,
  no-coda, sonorant, obstruent).
- **`id-natural-class`** (Round 2) — Q11 (coronals) is the
  textbook use case. The skill needs to handle the *narrowness*
  test — "you've got a class that includes the targets, but does
  it include other sounds that *aren't* in the data? If yes,
  narrow it."
- **`escalate-hint-lvl`** (Round 1) — HW5 ladder for syllabification:
  count vowels → assign nuclei → apply MOP → check sonority →
  assign moras. For Maltese: list environments per allomorph →
  identify the syllable-structural property that differs →
  propose UR → propose rule.
- **`repair-miscon`** (Round 1) — HW5-specific entries to add:
  "wider natural class is always better" (Q11), "shorter allomorph
  is the UR" (Q10), "assimilation only changes one feature" (Q12),
  "speech errors can swap any segments" (Q15), "all coda Cs are
  equal in weight" (Q17-Q18).

- **Q11 is the textbook `id-natural-class` test** — multiple
  feature labels (sonorants, voiced) include the target sounds,
  but only "coronals" is the narrowest exhaustive class. The skill
  has a documented use case to design against.

- **Q5-Q10 is the textbook `write-phon-rule` + UR-comparison
  pattern.** Two URs given in parallel, two rule sets formulated,
  the student picks based on simplicity. Rare in the course —
  the cleanest single test of Occam's razor.

- **The Farsi pluralization problem (Midterm 2 review C4) is the
  closest parallel to the Maltese problem.** Both test "competing
  UR analysis" reasoning. Worth introducing as warm-up.

- **The Dutch diminutive /-tjə/ (Zsiga ex. 9 KEY) is also a strong
  parallel** — multi-allomorph alternation, place assimilation as
  the dominant process, choice of UR by simplicity.

- **Q15 (speech errors) is conceptual, not arithmetic.** Tests
  the syllable-position-preservation hypothesis. Students who
  haven't internalized "onsets swap with onsets" will guess
  randomly. Worth a dedicated `escalate-hint-lvl` ladder.

- **Q17-Q18 (Greek moraic structure) is the cleanest mora test in
  the course.** A line from the Odyssey is given as data; students
  classify syllables as light/heavy/superheavy. Tests whether
  students can apply Greek mora rules (which differ from English).
