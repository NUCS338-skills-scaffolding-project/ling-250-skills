# HW2: Vowel Articulation, IPA, and Waveforms

## Unit / Days Covered

Days 4-5 of the course; Zsiga Ch. 3.

Days 1-3 anatomy, the IPA principle, and consonant articulation are
prerequisite and may be probed by `validate_pre_knowledge`.

## Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Describe English vowels in terms of tongue height, tongue
  backness, lip rounding, and tense/lax distinction
- Distinguish monophthongs from diphthongs and identify the five
  English diphthongs ([aɪ], [ɔɪ], [eɪ], [oʊ], [aʊ])
- Recognize the schwa/wedge distribution: schwa [ə] in unstressed
  syllables, wedge [ʌ] in stressed
- Apply the lax-vowel-not-word-final diagnostic
- Read the IPA vowel chart (height on vertical axis, backness on
  horizontal, paired vowels with rounded on the right)
- Distinguish between the vowel space (real articulatory area) and
  the vowel quadrilateral (idealized chart)
- Understand cardinal vowels as auditorily-equidistant reference
  points (NOT typological universals)
- Define and describe periodic vs aperiodic waves
- Apply wave terminology: cycle, wavelength, period, frequency
  (in Hz), amplitude
- Map physical to psychophysical: frequency↔pitch, amplitude↔loudness
- Understand fundamental frequency (F0) as the source signal and
  formants (F1, F2) as filter resonances
- Articulate the source-filter independence: same vowel at different
  F0 = same formants, different harmonic spacing
- Read amplitude (vertical) vs time (horizontal) on a waveform

## Concepts Required

### Building on Days 1-3 (prerequisite)

- Vocal tract anatomy: larynx, glottis, velum, oral cavity
- Voicing as vocal fold vibration vs. no vibration
- One-sound-one-symbol IPA principle
- Orthography ≠ pronunciation

### Day 4 — Vowels

- Vowel as syllable nucleus (unobstructed airflow)
- The two-step procedure for defining a vowel inventory: minimal
  pairs differing in vowel; identify dimensions of contrast
- Articulatory features: tongue height (high/low), tongue backness
  (front/back), lip rounding (rounded/unrounded), tense/lax,
  monophthong/diphthong
- English diphthongs: [aɪ] *bite*, [ɔɪ] *boy*, [eɪ] *bait*,
  [oʊ] *boat*, [aʊ] *bout*
- Schwa [ə] vs wedge [ʌ] convention (unstressed vs stressed)
- Lax vowels don't occur word-finally (except schwa)
- IPA vowel chart layout: vertical = height, horizontal = backness,
  paired = rounding (rounded on right)
- Vowel space (articulatory area) vs vowel quadrilateral (idealized)
- Cardinal vowels (Daniel Jones, 8 reference points) — auditorily
  equidistant, NOT typological universals
- Cross-linguistic vowel features that are non-contrastive in English:
  nasality, length, voice quality, tone

### Day 5 — Vowels wrap-up + Basic Acoustics

- Periodic vs aperiodic waves
- Pure tone (sinusoidal): fully described by frequency, amplitude,
  phase
- Wave terminology: cycle, wavelength, period (P), frequency
  (f = 1/P), amplitude
- Frequency (Hz) ↔ pitch (psychophysical perception)
- Amplitude ↔ loudness (psychophysical, mediated by intensity)
- Intensity (W/m²) — depends on amplitude AND frequency AND medium
- Decibel (dB) — logarithmic ratio scale
- Complex periodic waves — superpositions of multiple sinusoids
- Fundamental frequency (F0) — lowest frequency component, equals
  vocal fold vibration rate, perceived as pitch
- Harmonics — integer multiples of F0
- Formants — harmonics amplified by vocal tract resonance shape;
  F1 and F2 define vowel identity
- Source-filter theory:
  - Source = vibrating vocal folds (produces complex periodic wave)
  - Filter = vocal tract shape (selects which harmonics get amplified)
  - F0 and formants are independent
- Spectrum (spectral slice) = amplitude-vs-frequency at one moment;
  contrasted with waveform = amplitude-vs-time

## Question Types

- **Multi-select feature-based vowel identification** (Q1-Q6, 12 pts)
  — "Check all words that contain a [feature] vowel." One feature
  per question: high, low, front, back, rounded, lax. Word lists
  mix spelling-based traps with sound-based correct answers.

- **Diphthong matching** (Q7, 5 pts) — 10 words to 5 diphthong
  symbols. All five English diphthongs appear: [aʊ], [aɪ], [ɔɪ],
  [eɪ], [oʊ]. Word list: *frown, rye, foil, fray, loud, pays,
  proud, high, buy, show*.

- **IPA symbol → which word contains it** (Q8-Q17, 5 pts) — Given
  a vowel symbol, pick which of two words contains it. Word pairs
  designed to confuse via spelling (e.g., Q10 "[oʊ]: house vs so"
  — same "ou" letters, different vowels).

- **Vowel transformation under feature change** (Q18-Q22, 5 pts)
  — HW2's signature question type. Given a vowel, change one or
  two features, choose the result from the English vowel inventory.
  Examples: "Make [i] mid" → [e]; "Make [i] lax" → [ɪ]; "Make [ɑ]
  mid and lax" → [ʌ]. Tests the ability to navigate the vowel
  feature space holding constants and changing the named feature.

- **True/False on cardinal vowels** (Q23-Q25, 3 pts) — Tests the
  meaning of cardinal vowels as reference points (not as
  typological universals) and the auditory-equidistance property.

- **Open-ended sentence transcription** (Q26, 4 pts) — Student
  composes a meaningful English sentence with at least 8 different
  vowels, then transcribes the vowels in IPA. Diphthongs count as
  one vowel.

- **Waveform interpretation** (Q27-Q32, 6 pts) — Two waveforms
  shown; questions probe relative frequency, relative amplitude,
  relative perceived loudness, relative perceived pitch, x-axis
  label (Time), y-axis label (Amplitude). Tests both visual reading
  and the physical→psychophysical mapping.

## Common Misconceptions

Course-wide misconceptions from `context/misconceptions.md` especially
relevant to HW2:

- **Spelling reflects pronunciation** — central to every multi-select
  and transcription question. The English vowel system has
  particularly bad letter-sound correspondence (*tape* vs *tap*,
  *house* vs *so*, *good* as a back rounded vowel). Forces students
  to listen, not spell.
- **Tense and lax differ only in length** — students often think
  [i] vs [ɪ] is just "long i vs short i." It's a quality difference
  (tongue body position) plus length, with quality dominant. *Beat*
  and *bit* don't rhyme even at the same duration.
- **Schwa and wedge are different vowels** — they share quality
  (central-mid) but distribute by stress. Students who treat them
  as fully separate phonemes will over-distinguish.
- **Diphthongs are two vowels** — single vowels with changing quality;
  occupy one nucleus.
- **Lax vowels can occur word-finally** — they can't, in English
  (except schwa). Useful diagnostic for tense/lax classification
  when uncertain.
- **All languages have the same vowel features as English** — tone,
  nasality, length, and voice quality are contrastive in many
  languages but not in English.
- **The vowel chart shows literal tongue positions** — it's an
  idealized schematic. Real positions vary across speakers and
  languages.
- **Cardinal vowels are the most common vowel inventory in the
  world** — FALSE. The most common 5-vowel inventory is [i, e, u,
  o, a] (Spanish/Japanese-like). Cardinals are reference points,
  not typological universals. **High-priority catalog entry — tested
  on Quiz 1 Q3, Quiz 1 Q5, and HW2 Q23.**
- **Cardinal vowels 1-4 are not auditorily equidistant** — they ARE.
  Tested on HW2 Q24.
- **Amplitude equals loudness** — loudness is psychophysical,
  depending on intensity (which depends on amplitude AND frequency
  AND medium). Logarithmic, not linear relationship.
- **Frequency equals pitch** — same shape as amplitude/loudness.
  Roughly proportional but not identical, especially at extremes.
- **F0 and F1 are the same thing** — F0 is the fundamental frequency
  (rate of vocal fold vibration; perceived as pitch). F1 is the
  first formant (lowest amplified resonance of the vocal tract).
  Different physical quantities, different perceptual cues. **The
  single most-confused pair in vowel acoustics.**
- **Harmonics and formants are the same** — harmonics are integer
  multiples of F0 (source property). Formants are the *subset of
  harmonics* the vocal tract amplifies (filter property).
- **Frequency and amplitude are directly related** — they are
  **independent**. A 100 Hz sine can be loud or quiet; a 500 Hz
  sine can be loud or quiet. Tested on Make-up HW Q5.
- **A "complex wave" has a complicated F0-amplitude relationship**
  — a complex wave is **a wave made up of more than one frequency**.
  Speech is complex; pure tones are not.

Assignment-specific patterns to watch for:

- **Q10 ([oʊ] in "house" vs "so")**: spelling overlap trap. Both
  words use "ou"-style letters but have different vowels. Pure
  spelling-misconception failure.
- **Q18 ("Make [i] mid")**: students who pick [ə] instead of [e].
  The [ə] choice conflates "mid" with "central" or assumes mid +
  unstressed = schwa. Correct reasoning: [i] is front, so staying
  front and going from high to mid lands at [e]. **Prime
  `id_natural_class` moment.**
- **Q23 (cardinal vowels typology TF)**: students who think cardinals
  are the most common world vowels. The vast majority of inventories
  use sub/superset patterns of [i, e, u, o, a].
- **Q26 (open-ended sentence transcription)**: spelling-shape
  errors in unstressed syllables ("incl[u]ding" → IPA [u] instead
  of correct [ɪ]); "y" letter pulling toward [aɪ] regardless of
  context.
- **Q28 (amplitude reading)**: students who read amplitude as the
  horizontal extent of the wave instead of the vertical magnitude
  (peak-to-zero displacement).

## What Success Looks Like

A correct student:

- Treats each vowel symbol as a label for a sound, not a letter
- For multi-select feature questions, pronounces each word aloud
  (internally or externally), isolates the target vowel, and checks
  the relevant feature (height, backness, rounding, tense/lax)
- For "Make [X] feature-Y" questions, holds all other features
  constant and moves only along the Y dimension. E.g., "Make [i]
  mid" → keep front, keep tense, drop from high to mid → [e]
- For diphthong matching, identifies the vowel quality at the
  start and end of the diphthong before assigning the symbol
- For symbol-to-word questions, reads the IPA, says it aloud, then
  matches to the candidate word that produces that sound
- For waveform questions, distinguishes physical (frequency,
  amplitude) from psychophysical (pitch, loudness), and reads
  axis dimensions correctly (time on x, amplitude on y)
- Recognizes cardinal vowels as **reference points** for description,
  not as typologically common vowels

A student on the right track but not yet there typically:

- Gets vowel features right but second-guesses spelling-trap items
- Confuses tense/lax for stressed/unstressed
- Reads amplitude as horizontal extent of waveform
- Equates frequency with pitch literally (same value, no perceptual
  mapping)

## What the Tutor Should NOT Do

- **Do not transcribe student-composed sentences for Q26.** The
  student must produce each vowel themselves. The tutor can ask
  "what do you hear at the start of *enjoyed*?" but must never
  answer "the IPA for *enjoyed* is [ɛndʒɔɪd]."
- **Do not directly identify a vowel from a feature description
  before the student walks through height → backness → rounding
  → tense/lax** in order. This is the explicit `give_contrastive_hint`
  and `id_natural_class` move.
- **Do not name the feature that distinguishes two confused vowels
  before the student does.** Especially for [i]/[ɪ], [u]/[ʊ],
  [ɛ]/[æ], [ʌ]/[ə] — these are the workhorse contrastive pairs.
- **Do not tell the student which feature changes for Q18-Q22.**
  Walk them through the feature being changed and the features
  held constant.
- **Do not reveal F0, F1, F2 values from a spectrogram before
  student has identified peaks themselves** (HW3 territory but
  worth flagging).
- **Do not explain Praat usage in detail** — that's in the practicum,
  not HW2.
- **Do not introduce vowel-acoustic concepts (F0, formants, source-
  filter) on Q1-Q26 transcription questions** — they're vowel
  *articulation* questions. F0/formants are specifically for
  Q27-Q32.
- **Do not state that a particular cardinal vowel matches a
  particular language's vowels.** Cardinals are reference points,
  not language-internal categories.
- **Do not use vocabulary the student hasn't encountered yet.**
  Terms like *spectrum*, *spectrogram*, *VOT* are post-HW2 and
  shouldn't appear unless the student uses them first.

## Skills Relevant to This Assignment

- **`give-contrastive-hint`** — workhorse for tense/lax pairs
  ([i]/[ɪ], [u]/[ʊ], [eɪ]/[ɛ], [oʊ]/[ɔ]) and front/back contrasts.
  Especially useful for Q1-Q6 (multi-select feature identification).
- **`id_natural_class`** (Round 2) — Q18-Q22 are the textbook use
  case. Students need to navigate the vowel feature space holding
  constants and changing one named feature.
- **`probe_min_pair`** (Round 2) — minor use; mostly for tense/lax
  and front/back contrast cases ("can you think of a minimal pair
  that distinguishes [i] and [ɪ]?").
- **`repair_misconceptions`** — fires on cardinal vowel typology
  errors (Q23-Q24), schwa/wedge inversions, frequency-amplitude
  conflation, F0/F1 confusion, and any spelling-driven transcription
  error.
- **`escalate_hint_level_gradually`** — fires on Q26 (composed
  sentence transcription) when the student gets stuck after
  initial questioning. Hint ladder: "say the word slowly →
  identify the stressed syllable → identify the vowel quality
  at that syllable → match to vowel chart features."
- **`validate_pre_knowledge`** — fires before the student begins.
  Probes Days 1-3 anatomy/IPA principle plus Day 4 vowel chart
  structure.
- **`diagnose_prerequisite_gaps`** — fires when the student is
  blocked on a question. Common gaps: vowel chart axis confusion,
  tense/lax binary, monophthong/diphthong distinction.

## Notes for the Instructor

- **Q18-Q22 (vowel transformation under feature change) is the
  highest-leverage HW2 content for the new Round 2 skill
  `id_natural_class`.** Students who don't internalize "hold
  constants, change one feature" will struggle with HW4 natural
  class identification and HW5 sonority hierarchy questions.
  Worth targeted pre-work using these as practice.

- **Q23-Q24 (cardinal vowel typology and equidistance) test a
  specific pair of misconceptions repeatedly** — Quiz 1 Q3 and Q5
  test the same content. Pattern: students assume cardinal vowels
  describe the most common world inventory. Worth a dedicated
  catalog-and-repair pass before the assignment.

- **The waveform questions Q27-Q32 are HW2's bridge to HW3.**
  Students who can read amplitude vs time and match physical to
  psychophysical correctly will be set up for HW3's spectrogram
  reading. Students who can't will struggle on HW3 Q1-Q9.

- **Open-ended Q26 is the hardest to grade and the most pedagogically
  rich** — students compose their own data, so error patterns are
  individual. Worth tracking common errors per student to build
  a personal misconceptions catalog.
