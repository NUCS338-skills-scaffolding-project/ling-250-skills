# HW4: Phonemic Analysis & Alternations

## Unit / Days Covered

Days 8-13 of the course. Days 8-9 are practica/in-class assessment;
conceptual content is concentrated in Days 11-13. Day 11 is the
phonemic principle core. Day 12 introduces phonological processes.
Day 13 covers morpho-phonological alternations and allomorph selection.

Days 1-7 phonetic features (place, manner, voicing for consonants;
height, backness, rounding, tense/lax for vowels) are prerequisite
for natural class reasoning and may be probed by `validate-prereqs`.

This is the most content-heavy unit of the course and the assignment
that synthesizes the most material. Spend time on it.

## Learning Goals

Students should be able to:

- Distinguish phonetics (physical, articulation, acoustics) from
  phonology (mental representation, sound categories, processes)
- Distinguish underlying/phonemic representation (slashes /X/) from
  surface/phonetic representation (brackets [X])
- Define and apply: phoneme (lexically contrastive sound), allophone
  (predictable variant of a phoneme), free variation (interchangeable
  variants in the same context)
- Identify minimal pairs as evidence of phonemic contrast
- Conduct distributional analysis: list environments, classify as
  overlapping vs complementary
- Apply the phonemic principle: complementary distribution + phonetic
  similarity → allophones; overlapping distribution → separate phonemes
- Choose the underlying representation by simplicity (Occam's razor):
  pick the form yielding fewer/simpler rules
- Write phonological rules in /X/ → [Y] / __ [environment] notation
- Use natural-class features rather than listing individual sounds in
  rule environments
- Identify the five families of phonological processes: assimilation,
  dissimilation, fortition/lenition, epenthesis/deletion, metathesis
- Distinguish allophone (phoneme variant) from allomorph (morpheme
  variant)
- Analyze allomorph selection paradigms: identify variants, list
  environments, choose UR by simplicity, identify the process
- Apply phonological rules to underlying representations to derive
  surface forms (forward derivation)

## Concepts Required

### Building on Days 1-7 (prerequisite)

- IPA symbols and articulatory features (place, manner, voicing for
  consonants; height, backness, rounding, tense/lax for vowels)
- Source-filter theory (briefly relevant for natural class
  reasoning around F0 / formants)

### Day 11 — The Phonemic Principle

- Phonetics vs phonology
- Two levels of representation:
  - Phonemic / underlying / abstract — written /slashes/, mental,
    context-insensitive
  - Phonetic / surface / concrete — written [brackets], real,
    context-sensitive
- Phoneme — abstract category, lexically contrastive
- Allophone — predictable variant, physically real
- The English /p/ example: [pʰ p p˺] all → /p/; [b b̥] both → /b/
- Tests for phonemic status:
  - Overlapping distribution → separate phonemes
  - Complementary distribution + phonetic similarity → allophones
- Minimal pair, minimal set
- Distributional analysis methodology:
  1. Hypothesize about two sounds
  2. List environments (left + right adjacent, word boundaries)
  3. Compare; classify as overlapping or complementary
- Phonetic similarity condition (counterexample: English [h]/[ŋ])
- UR selection: broader/elsewhere distribution; simplest rules
- Spanish /b/ vs /β/ example (lenition: /b/ → [β] / V_V)
- Phonological rule notation:
  ```
  /A/ → [B] / X __ Y
  ```
  with `#` = word boundary, `V`/`N`/`C` = natural class shorthand,
  `[+feature]` = feature-based class, `Ø` = null

### Day 12 — Phonological Processes (taxonomy)

Five families:

1. **Feature-changing:**
   - Assimilation — make adjacent sounds more similar (English vowel
     nasalization, English plural voicing)
   - Dissimilation — make sounds less similar (Latin -alis → -aris)

2. **Sonority-changing:**
   - Strengthening / fortition — make stronger (Porteño Spanish [j] → [ʒ])
   - Weakening / lenition — make weaker (English flapping /t,d/ → [ɾ])

3. **Insertion/deletion:**
   - Epenthesis — add a sound (English [p] in *warmth*; loanword
     repair *al-jabr* → *algebra*)
   - Deletion — remove a sound (English [t] in *best game*)

4. **Reordering:**
   - Metathesis — swap two sounds (Spanish *miraculum* → *milagro*)

### Day 13 — Morpho-Phonological Alternations

- Morpheme: smallest meaningful unit
- Allomorph: variant of a morpheme (vs allophone = variant of a phoneme)
- The English plural [-s], [-z], [-əz] paradigm
- The English past tense [-d], [-t], [-əd] paradigm
- The English in-/im-/il-/ir- prefix paradigm (the canonical
  multi-allomorph case)
- Allomorph selection methodology:
  1. List variants
  2. List contexts each appears in
  3. Generalize over contexts (find natural classes)
  4. Choose UR by simplicity
  5. Identify the process
- Phonotactic constraints — language-specific rules about which
  sounds can be adjacent
- Natural classes: groups of sounds sharing features AND exhausting
  the sounds with those features

### Cross-cutting (Days 11-13)

- Distributional analysis is the unified methodology that runs
  through phoneme/allophone questions, alternation rule writing,
  and allomorph selection
- Free variation as a third category alongside phoneme and allophone
  (variants interchangeable in the same context)

## Question Types

- **Distributional analysis (collect contexts)** — Given language
  data (10-15 forms), list contexts (left/right environments) for
  two target sounds.

- **Rule writing** — Given an identified alternation, write the
  rule in formal notation. Multiple parts of the assignment.

- **Natural class identification** — Given a set of sounds,
  identify the feature(s) that unite them as the narrowest
  exhaustive class.

- **Phonemic analysis (full diagnostic)** — For each sound pair
  in each language, walk through the full Phonemic Principle:
  minimal pairs? distribution? phonetic similarity? UR? rule(s)?

- **Minimal pair identification** — Whether minimal pairs exist
  or not, and what they prove.

- **UR identification** — Given an alternation, identify which
  form is the underlying representation. Tests Occam's razor.

- **Cross-linguistic phonological status questions** — Given
  data, determine the phonemic status of two sounds in a *specific
  language*. The same sounds can be phonemes in one language and
  allophones in another.

## Common Misconceptions

Course-wide misconceptions especially relevant to HW4:

### Phoneme/allophone-related

- **Two sounds with the same IPA in two different languages are
  the same phoneme.** No — phonemes are language-specific
  categorizations. [p] and [pʰ] are the same phoneme in English
  but different phonemes in Hindi.
- **Phonemes are "real" sounds and allophones are weird variations.**
  INVERTED. **Allophones are physically real** (the actual sounds
  spoken/heard). **Phonemes are mental abstractions** that group
  allophones together.
- **[i]/[ɪ] are allophones in English.** ⭐ FALSE — they are
  separate phonemes. Minimal pairs: *bit*/*beat*, *sit*/*seat*,
  *fit*/*feet*. They're allophones in some other languages
  (Spanish has only /i/), but in English they contrast.
  **Catalog as the highest-priority misconception for HW4.**
  Students project English categorizations onto other languages
  and vice versa.
- **If two sounds appear in similar contexts, they must be allophones.**
  No — that would mean every contrastive pair is allophones. The
  diagnostic is *complementary* distribution; overlap → phonemes.
- **Complementary distribution means the same word can have either
  sound.** No — complementary means the sounds occur in *different*
  contexts. Swapping one for the other yields a non-word or unusual
  pronunciation, not a different word.
- **Phonetic similarity isn't important — only distribution matters.**
  False. Phonetic similarity is part of the phonemic principle.
  English [h] and [ŋ] are in complementary distribution but are
  NOT allophones — they fail the similarity test.
- **Minimal pairs are required for two sounds to be phonemes.**
  No — minimal pairs are *clear evidence* of overlapping
  distribution, but overlapping is the actual diagnostic. Hindi
  [b]/[b̤] are phonemes shown by overlapping environments
  (#__a, #__i), not strict minimal pairs.
- **The underlying form is the way the word is spelled.** Spelling
  has nothing to do with the UR. Determined by distributional
  analysis.
- **Whichever allophone appears most often is the UR.** Not always.
  The UR is the one with **broadest distribution** (the elsewhere
  case), which usually correlates with frequency but not always.
- **A phoneme is a sound that never appears in a minimal pair.**
  FALSE (Quiz 4 Q5). A phoneme is a *lexically contrastive sound*.
  Minimal pairs are evidence FOR phonemic status.
- **Free variation is the same as allophony.** Different categories.
  Allophones are positionally restricted (predictable). Free
  variants are interchangeable in the same context. Example:
  word-final [t] in English can be [t] or [t˺] — same context,
  both acceptable, free variation.

### Rule-writing-related

- **Phonological rules describe physical articulations.** Rules
  are abstract symbolic mappings between underlying and surface
  representations.
- **Slashes and brackets are interchangeable.** They're NOT.
  /X/ = phoneme/UR; [X] = surface form. Mixing them in a single
  rule is a notational error signaling level-of-representation
  confusion.
- **You can list any group of sounds as a "natural class."** A
  natural class must (1) share a feature, AND (2) exhaust the
  sounds in the language with that feature. [p l w] is not a
  class — many other consonants share their commonalities.
- **All processes are assimilation.** Five distinct families:
  assimilation, dissimilation, fortition/lenition,
  epenthesis/deletion, metathesis.

### Allomorph-related

- **Allophones and allomorphs are the same thing.** Different units.
  Allophones = variants of a phoneme. Allomorphs = variants of a
  morpheme.
- **The shorter allomorph is always the UR.** No. UR is by
  broadest distribution, not shortest form. English plural [-z]
  is the UR even though [-s] is also short. Negative prefix /ɪn-/
  is the UR even though [ɪl-], [ɪɹ-], [ɪm-] are all the same length.
- **Every alternation is voicing assimilation.** Voicing assimilation
  is one common process. Alternations can also be place assimilation
  (negative prefix), epenthesis (English -əz), deletion (English
  t/d-deletion), or metathesis.
- **Stem-and-affix sequences pronounce as their literal concatenation.**
  Often they do, but when they violate phonotactic constraints,
  the language repairs the form.

### Methodological misconceptions

- **The data tells you the answer immediately.** No —
  distributional analysis is *iterative*. List the contexts, look
  for patterns, refine the rule.
- **There's only one correct UR.** Sometimes both candidate URs
  are technically valid; the choice is by simplicity (Occam's
  razor). HW5's Maltese problem and Midterm 2's Farsi problem
  both test this.

Assignment-specific patterns to watch for:

- **Projecting English categorizations onto other languages.**
  The central error pattern. [i]/[ɪ], [s]/[ʃ], [t]/[d] may be
  phonemes in English but allophones somewhere else (or vice
  versa). Students must work from the data given, not from
  intuition.
- **Skipping distributional analysis and jumping to a conclusion.**
  Common when students think they "see" a pattern. The discipline
  of writing out every environment is what catches the analysis.
- **Confusing complementary and overlapping distribution.**
  Complementary = NEVER the same context. Overlapping = at least
  one shared context.
- **Phonetic similarity overlooked.** Especially when complementary
  distribution is obvious. Hindi [h]/[ŋ] is the textbook
  counterexample.
- **Listing sounds where features should be used in rules.** A
  student who writes `/[b/d/g]/ → [β/ð/ɣ] / V_V` is missing the
  natural-class generalization (`/[+voiced stop]/ → [+fricative]
  / V_V`).
- **Picking the wrong UR.** Students often pick the form they see
  most in the data, or the "default" English form, rather than
  the form yielding simplest derivation. Walk through both
  candidates, count rules required.
- **Mixing slashes and brackets.** Notational confusion signals
  conceptual confusion about levels.
- **Mixing phoneme labels and underlying forms.** "The phoneme
  /pʰ/" is wrong — the phoneme is /p/ even though [pʰ] is one
  of its allophones.

## What Success Looks Like

A correct student:

- Approaches each language's data on its own terms, not via
  English projection
- Writes out every environment (left + right adjacent, word
  boundaries) before drawing conclusions
- Distinguishes overlapping from complementary distribution by
  literal comparison of context lists
- Checks phonetic similarity even when complementary distribution
  is clear
- Walks through both candidate URs (one per allophone) before
  picking the one that yields simpler rules
- Uses natural-class features in rule environments rather than
  listing specific segments
- Maintains slashes/brackets distinction throughout the analysis
- Identifies the process family (assimilation, lenition, etc.)
  before writing the rule
- For allomorph paradigms, treats them as parallel to phoneme
  analysis: list variants, list contexts, find the natural class,
  choose UR by simplicity
- For forward rule application: checks whether the rule's
  environment matches the UR; if yes, applies the change; if no,
  the rule doesn't apply

A student on the right track but not yet there typically:

- Lists environments correctly but mis-classifies overlapping vs
  complementary
- Identifies the right phoneme/allophone status but writes the
  rule with sounds instead of features
- Picks an UR but can't justify the choice
- Identifies the process family but mis-writes the formal rule

## What the Tutor Should NOT Do

- **Do not classify sounds (phonemes vs allophones) before the
  student has done the distributional analysis themselves.**
- **Do not propose the UR before the student has compared both
  candidate analyses.**
- **Do not supply the natural-class label** before the student
  has listed the shared features. (`id-natural-class` skill is
  precisely about NOT doing this.)
- **Do not write the rule for the student** — guide them through
  change, description, environment, and class.
- **Do not reveal whether two sounds are phonemes in a *specific*
  language by appealing to English.** Each language stands on its
  own.
- **Do not tell the student which type of process is at work**
  (assimilation, lenition, etc.) before they've described what's
  changing.
- **Do not transcribe the rule in formal notation if the student
  hasn't tried.** The notational fluency is the skill being tested.
- **Do not mix slashes and brackets in your own examples** —
  model the distinction faithfully.

4 of 5 target this HW directly:

- **`analyze-dist`** (Round 2) — workhorse. Every distributional

  propose UR.
- **`probe-min-pair`** (Round 2) — for "are these phonemes?"

  absence of minimal pairs is informative vs just small dataset.

  Scaffold the slashes/brackets distinction, the structural change,
  the environment, the natural-class generalization. **Bidirectional**:
  cover both writing-from-data (HW4) and applying-to-UR (Midterm 2
  review).
- **`id-natural-class`** (Round 2) — for natural class questions
  (and embedded in every rule-writing question). Coach the feature
  analysis. The NARROWNESS test: "you've got a class that includes
  the targets, but does it include other sounds that *aren't* in
  the data? If yes, narrow it."
- **`repair-miscon`** (Round 1, extended) — heavy use.
  Misconceptions to add: [i]/[ɪ] cross-linguistic error, allophone
  vs allomorph, slashes/brackets, phoneme labels, "free variation
  = allophone."
- **`contrastive-hint`** (Round 1) — for differentiating
  overlapping from complementary distribution; for differentiating
  phoneme from allophone status.
- **`escalate-hint-lvl`** (Round 1) — heavily used; HW4 is
  content-dense and students get stuck often. Pre-built scaffolds:

  assignment. Worth scaffolding more time than other HWs.
